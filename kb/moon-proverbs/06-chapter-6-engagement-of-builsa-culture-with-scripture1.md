Often, a consistent epistemology is not applied for both Scripture and culture;  
rather, these two factors are often applied in an *inverse relationship.* For example, those  
who wanted to ignore the drum ban maintained a Naive Realist view of Scripture  
\(Scripture is totally objective and we need to obey Psalms 150 to the letter\), while also  
holding an Instrumentalist view of culture \(culture is totally subjective and can be  
changed or ignored\). The inconsistent epistemology applied to the interaction of Scripture  
and culture provides insight into the confusion that occurs. Let us look at how mission  
practice has often combined the two factors of Scripture and culture in order to more  
clearly see this inconsistency and learn from it.  
As the Scripture and culture interact across the length of this continuum, five  
common areas are identified. It is helpful to think of these areas as common resting  
places and not fixed end points since this interaction of the Scripture and culture is an  
ongoing process. This chart reveals the underlying epistemological assumptions for the  
views held for the drumming ban discussed above. The five common resting places can  
be described as follows\:  
**1\. Acceptance** = This is a Naive Realist view of culture together with an  
Instrumentalist view of Scripture. Culture supercedes Scripture or deals with issues that  
Scripture does not address. All cultural practices are seen as valid. Scriptural knowledge  
is seen as totally subjective. This leads to cultural relativism and theological  
universalism. Pastor Kofi holds this position.  
**2\. Absorption** = This is a tendency towards \(though not a total commitment to\) a  
Naive Realist view of culture and an Instrumentalist view of Scripture. Culture tends to  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. 201  
interpret Scripture. Cultural practices are absorbed into the life of the church with very  
little change. This leads to assimilation. Scripture is like cheesecloth that strains out large  
pieces but allows much of the cultural liquid to flow through unchanged. The practical  
outcome is often syncretism.  
**3\. Critical Engagement=** This is a Critical Realist view of both Scripture and  
culture. An example is the mother tongue Scripture, which contains both the Scripture  
and language of culture; therefore, it becomes the truth that is the interpreter of culture. In  
addition, the culture is actively engaged with the Scripture allowing for a creative tension  
that releases new insight into both the culture and the Scripture. Both Scripture and  
culture receive new appreciation as the process of engagement draws out the best from  
each. It is not a mere accommodation or absorption of cultural practices; rather, this  
engagement of the mother tongue Scripture reveals the glory of God in ways that have  
been hidden in the past. This view does not have a low view of Scripture; it merely  
recognizes the human limitations of language resulting in an understanding of Scripture  
that is not yet complete. A fuller understanding comes when the Holy Spirit speaks  
through the creative interaction of various cultures and the mother tongue Scripture.  
Scriptural knowledge is then both objective and subjective, in accordance with I  
Corinthians 13\:12 \(“Now I know in part; then I shall know fully”\). The practical outcome  
is proper contextualization resulting in indigenous Christianity.  
**4\. Modification** = This view tends toward \(though does not commit to\) a Naive  
Realist view of Scripture and an Instrumentalist view of culture. The Scripture tends to be  
the final interpreter of culture. Culture engages the Scripture in “familiar territory,  
allowing modifications to areas such as music, dress, worship style, language, etc. Since

the engagement of the two is only partial, only the categories and themes that are familiar  
to the traditional church are treated. This allows the modification of practices that are  
“safe” but no new insight is generally gained. The practical outcome is often a “foreign  
gospel” \(e.g., Western theology in African clothing\).  
**5\. Rejection** = This views holds a Naive Realist view of Scripture and an  
Instrumentalist view of culture. Scripture critiques all culture as evil. The culture is  
prejudged to be unredeemable; therefore, there is no engagement of the two - simply  
rejection of the culture. This leads to the *“tabula rasa"* doctrine. Some have tried to  
maintain this position by removing themselves from cultural practices and not becoming  
engaged in them. This implies that the Scripture is fully understood and totally objective.  
This perspective is captured by those who say, “The Bible says what it means, and means  
what it says. Period.” Since knowledge of Scripture is seen to be timeless and universal,  
there is no other interpretation that will be considered. The practical outcome is often  
“split-level” Christianity, isolation, or Fundamentalism. Pastor David holds this position.  
In light of this continuum, the target or goal for the interaction of Scripture and  
culture is brought to focus. While the other positions on the continuum reflect  
inconsistent epistemologies resulting in confusion, critical engagement \(area 3 above\)  
maintains a consistent Critical Realist epistemology for both culture and Scripture,  
thereby providing clarity. Proper contextualization of Christianity will occur as a result of  
the process of critical engagement. By critically engaging the Scripture with the culture,  
both the image of God and the stain of sin will be revealed in the culture. Our approach to  
mission will have cultural relevance with biblical integrity. In addition, the Scripture will  
be further understood and appreciated by those within the culture. This Critical Realist

this understanding and approach will be demonstrated by the drum ban in Ghana.  
Pastor David and others who argued that we should obey God in Psalms 150 and  
and ignore the *teng nyono* demonstrate a Naive Realist view of Scripture, whereby  
Scripture is a picture of reality that is fully understood apart from varying cultural  
contexts. At the same time, they held an Instrumentalist epistemology concerning culture,  
whereby culture can be readily ignored or changed. By combining the epistemologies of  
Scripture and culture, this position can be plotted on the “Interaction of Scripture and

Pastor Kofi and others who felt we should uncritically heed the *teng nyono*’s  
words for this short period of time demonstrate a Nai ve Realist view of culture, where  
culture is a picture of reality that should not be challenged. They also have an  
Instrumentalist view of Scripture since they felt that Scripture could be readily ignored or  
dismissed. The combination of the epistemologies of Scripture and culture can be plotted  
on the continuum on the far left in the position of Acceptance \(area 1\).  
By plotting these positions on the continuum, it shows the two extreme positions.  
The continuum helps to see which direction each position needs to consider moving

Before the Friday meeting, let us reflect on what has been happening. Note how  
this case falls into the “excluded middle.” Joe had never discussed God’s use of and  
control of wind in any of his seminary training. He never learned about the *teng nyono*  
either. As a result, he did not know how Scripture could address the case. It was not until  
they started to dig to the roots that they realized how Scripture does speak powerfully to  
the case. Once the roots are discovered, then a spiritual response can be given. Note how

Let us pause here to reflect on what has happened with the *teng nyono.* Consider  
the issues that are being dealt with - health, blessing on the crop, safety on a journey.  
These are all “excluded middle” issues. Previously, the *teng nyono* would not look to the  
church for help for these spiritual issues. Now, he turns to them. The church responds in a  
way that is culturally relevant and Scripturally faithful, and God responds. How did we  
get to this point?  
There are no easy answers or quick formulas for dealing with issues like the  
banning of drums in Ghana. The common default responses of rejection or acceptance  
limit the discussion and creative energy that could be released by the Holy Spirit. When  
discussing historical mission practices in Africa on issues such as polygamy, bride  
wealth, circumcision, etc., Hastings points out that, rather than being an obstacle, in the  
long run, these issues have been an instrument to focus on what being African and  
Christian means. He concludes, “Christianity has often thrived upon internal argument”  
\(1994\:322\). In other words, authentic expressions of African Christianity thrive upon the  
arguments \(dialogue\) created when Scripture and culture are critically engaged.  
In analyzing the narrative, several key elements emerge. First of all, note how the  
process was facilitated by the catalysts, Joe and Immanuel. They were the ones who  
called the leaders together. If not, the church leaders would have likely remained in their  
initial positions of acceptance or rejection. The catalysts also facilitated the process by  
consulting with others outside the group. They were able to provide the etic perspective  
through this interaction with church leaders from other people groups who were removed  
from the original context. In this case, the two catalysts, Joe and Immanuel were ideal  
since Immanuel could offer the emic perspective while Joe added the etic perspective. At

This narrative also shows the importance of involving a group in the process of

The role of the Holy Spirit can not be underplayed. Contextualization does not

to Christian experience.” Acknowledging this promotes a humility that God is at work in  
other Christian’s lives, regardless of their education levels, positions, experience, etc.

epistemology.

Chapter Summary  
The narrative revealed several necessary ingredients to promote contextualization.  
By way of summary, these include.  
1\. There must be a deep conviction that the mother tongue Scripture is the word of  
God that is the ultimate “interpreter of culture and tradition” \(Bediako 1999a\: 1\) since it  
provides the clearest revelation of God in culture.  
2\. Space and time are needed for the Holy Spirit to speak to the issues of Scripture  
and culture, realizing that the Holy Spirit is more interested in this process than we are  
since it ultimately reveals the glory of God.  
3\. Humility must be paramount in order to accept our limited understanding of  
both Scripture and culture. This motivates one to be open to learning about areas of  
Scripture that have been overlooked. In addition, it persuades one to learn from the  
culture via the people, proverbs, and powers such as the *teng nyono*, etc.  
4\. Contextualization is best done in a hermeneutical community. This includes  
listening to the local, regional, and global church, as well as those within the culture who  
are the repositories of sacred wisdom or authority. Both the etic and emic perspectives  
provide valuable insight for contextualization.
