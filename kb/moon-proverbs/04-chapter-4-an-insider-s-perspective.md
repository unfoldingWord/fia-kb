This encounter reveals the types of struggles that Builsa Christians experience.  
While they are sincere in their faith, they are uncertain how Scripture would help them to  
respond to the “middle level issues” that they face. Immanuel represents a typical Builsa  
Christian. While he is not an actual person, his responses and actions were drawn from  
several actual Builsa; therefore, he is a composite person representing many actual cases.  
The narrative also portrays common responses that Builsa Christians often give  
concerning issues relating to Builsa culture. Often times, the quick answer is just to reject  
the culture and have nothing to do with the ancestors and their ways. After all, this may  
cause people to fall back into African primal religion. Some even say that the entire  
Builsa culture is full of demonic activity and it must all be cleansed. If one tries to remain

Immanuel’s tragedy is an example of what Hiebert has called “The excluded  
middle,” since it is “one of the greatest cultural gaps between Western people and many  
traditional religionists” \(Hiebert 1994\:196\). The excluded middle deals with issues  
arising from primal religion that include entities at work in this world that are beyond  
rational explanation since they are unseen or supernatural, such as ancestors, ghosts,  
spirits, witches, local gods or goddesses, etc. The key issues that this level addresses are  
the causes of death and identity in life, well-being and misfortune, guidance for an  
uncertain future, and justice amidst evil \(Hiebert et al. 1999\:74\). All of these issues are  
involved in this case to some degree. Unfortunately, typical Western missionaries and

Africans are also crying out for a further understanding of African primal religion.  
African theologian, Bediako \(1989\:59\), states, “There is probably no issue more crucial  
than the need to understand this heightened interest in the African pre-Christian religious  
tradition if Africa’s theologians are to be interpreted correctly and their achievement duly  
recognized.” Turaki \(2000\:281\) also affirms the need for understanding primal religion,  
The most crucial theological issue at hand is that most African Christians lack  
sufficient knowledge of the Bible and its teaching on African traditional religions  
and cultures... .Even though the influence of the African traditional religions and  
cultures is profound in modem Africa, most African Christians have little  
knowledge of how this influence is exerted.  
The tragedy is that Immanuel’s dilemma is not an isolated case; rather, it is  
representative of the many primal religion issues that Builsa face. Since the Builsa are not  
accustomed to engaging Buli Scripture with the Builsa culture, it is just too difficult a hill  
to climb. As a result, they often fall back on the two responses of either total acceptance  
\(as in Pastor Kofi\) or total rejection \(as in Pastor David\). There is very little engagement  
of the Scripture with Builsa culture since the process is just too difficult.

The difficulty described above is similar to learning to ride a bicycle. In the  
beginning, they are taught to ride on smooth, flat ground until they get used to the  
motion. After practice, they are ready to take on the challenge of climbing a hill. If they  
were taught to ride the bike by going up the hill initially, then the challenge would be too  
great and they would likely give up - it is just too difficult.  
To ask Immanuel to engage Buli Scripture with the issue of the “dirt from his  
son’s grave” is similar to asking him to learn to ride a bike by going up a hill. He has not  
been taught the process of developing contextual theology, and he has not had practice  
applying it on easier issues. When this difficult issue is then given to him, he is not  
prepared. Pastors Kofi and David have been taught the Scripture, but they have also not  
been taught how to ride the bike of contextual theology. As a result, this is just too  
difficult a hill to climb. The easy and familiar responses are either total rejection or total  
acceptance.  
Clearly, the Builsa need to learn how to ride the bike of contextual theology. If  
they can get used to riding this bike on easy ground dealing with issues that they can  
tackle more readily, then they can later be ready to deal with the difficult cultural issues  
that emerge. Where is this easy ground? For the Builsa, their own proverbs are familiar,  
easy ground. They love to use proverbs in conversation and reason with them. The Builsa  
can reflect on their own proverbs and dig out the roots to find the cultural values. When  
using the Buli Scripture as the interpreter to find the roots that are proper for a Christian  
and those that were not, they are engaging Scripture with Builsa culture on easy ground.  
Once they are used to riding the bike, then they can climb the uphill challenge of  
reflecting on the ‘dirt from the grave’ issue.

Before proceeding with Immanuel’s story, this encounter reveals several insights.  
The value of proverbs is easily overlooked by Westerners who come from cultures with  
high literacy. In primary oral cultures like the Builsa culture, however, proverbs retain a  
prominent position. When 108 Builsa were asked why they speak in proverbs, over one  
quarter responded, “This is the type of speech we like. It brings laughter and enjoyment  
into the conversation” \(Moon 2000\). Communication in Africa is an art form\: how you  
speak is just as important as what you say. Ong observed, “Oral cultures indeed produce  
powerful and beautiful verbal performances of high artistic value and human worth”  
\(1982\:14\). This artistic value can be easily overlooked by missionaries. A Nigerian pastor

once told me, “If one does not speak in proverbs, he will not carry people with him for  
long - they will walk for a short way only and then leave him. If he speaks in proverbs,  
however, the people will not be able to close their ears!” Immanuel was drawn to the  
proverbs meeting since it was entertaining. This became a hook for him to engage the  
Scripture with his culture.  
It should also be noted that the logic pattern of primary oral cultures is different  
than that of cultures with high literacy. Ong \(1982\) shows that oral cultures tend to be  
elaborate in thought but this thought is organized and expressed in a way that is foreign to  
the literate mind. Ong notes, “More than any other single invention, writing has  
transformed human consciousness” \(1982\:78\). Overall, oral thinking is formulaic and  
structured in proverbs and other set expressions. Notice how the meaning of the first  
proverb was described by giving another proverb. Serious thought is intertwined with  
memory systems and these mnemonics, such as proverbs and stories, form the basis of  
thought itself. Ong continues, “You know what you can recall... In a primary oral  
culture, to solve effectively the problem of retaining and retrieving carefully articulated  
thought, you have to do your thinking in mnemonic patterns, shaped for ready oral  
recurrence” \(1982\:33-34\). The discussion of the proverb was stitched together by several  
other proverbs that could be readily recalled. This logic is different than the linear  
propositional logic that literate cultures tend to prefer.  
The logic of the Buli word *“magsi”* deserves some explanation. When I first  
discussed with some of the church leaders about critiquing proverbs, I discussed how we  
wanted to accept the “*wensie*” \(truth\) and reject the “*venta*” \(falsehood\). One pastor  
observantly approached me later and suggested that perhaps we should find other terms

to use. These terms were too harsh and it pushed them back into rigid categories of full  
acceptance or full rejection. This was the opposite of what we were trying to accomplish.  
As a result, he suggested the term *“magsi”* This word means “to be proper, fitting,

adjustment to cultural issues but does not force the decision into black and white

we want to discover what is proper, fitting, suitable, or right in the Builsa culture from  
God’s perspective, as revealed in Scripture.  
It is also interesting to observe how the proverbs discussion opened up a door to  
talk about a wide variety of cultural issues. Notice how the topic of witchcraft was  
brought into the discussion. While this is an ever-present reality in the Builsa area, it is  
not often discussed in the church. By taking the proverbs seriously, it opened the way for  
them to freely discuss cultural roots that are often not addressed. This prepared the  
ground for contextualization. Also notice how the proverbs discussion encouraged  
Immanuel to bring forth his heart-felt tragedy for discussion in the church. He was  
beginning to feel that it was safe to discuss this issue. While this heartfelt issue was  
foremost in his mind, it would often not be addressed in church. It would be part of the  
“excluded middle” that Scripture seems not to address. After the proverbs discussion,  
however, Immanuel observed how Scripture addressed the meaning of the first proverb.  
This encouraged him to think that perhaps Scripture will speak to his issue, like it did  
with the proverb. Immanuel was starting to learn how to ride the bike. If he persists and  
rides this bike enough on ‘easy ground,’ then he will be able to tackle the difficult hills.

While the church leaders are on break, let us reflect on what transpired. There are  
aspects of culture that are hard to explain and describe. Notice how several of the church  
leaders did not want to discuss a certain proverb initially. The proverbs functioned as  
symbols to provide a window into the culture that would otherwise be closed. Tillich  
described how symbols “open up levels of reality which otherwise are closed” and that  
they “open up levels of the human mind of which we otherwise are not aware”  
\(1955\:109\). This is crucial for contextualization to occur since hidden aspects of culture  
need to be understood before they can be engaged with Scripture. The proverbs serve to  
bring the issues to the surface so that the roots can be explored.  
As a result of exploring the window of proverbs, a world of discovery exploded  
open for the church leaders. The value of proverbs in this discovery process is affirmed  
by the Yoruba proverb, “Owe *I ’esin oro; bi oro ba nu, owe ni a f i nwa a”* **\(A proverb is a**  
**horse which can carry you swiftly to the discovery of ideas sought\)** \(Delano  
1976\:Foreward\). The church leaders were able to dig into the roots of the proverb in  
order to find Builsa cultural values. It is at the level of the roots that the Scripture can  
most actively engage the culture resulting in transformation. Often times, discussions  
center around aspects of the tree without digging into the roots. As will be shown later,

when people focus only on the tree \(aspects that can be seen such as ugly people, a man

and not permissible. This imagery helped the group to feel and understand the elders’

that “the use of traditional forms of oral art would help many West African audiences to  
hear and study Bible portions” \(1982\:125\). The richness of metaphor in oral art is highly  
valued and appreciated in the Builsa culture, which stimulated further creativity in  
resolving the issues underneath this situation.

Chapter Summary  
Through the above narrative, we get an inside look at the struggles of the Builsa Christian  
community and how the serious study of proverbs can be helpful. Some of the highlights  
that deserve repeat notice are\:  
1\. Middle level issues are not easy to address. The common responses are either to reject  
culture completely or to accept it completely. This leads to little, if any, engagement of  
the Scripture with the culture, thereby leading to Split-level Christianity.  
2\. Since it is difficult to engage Scripture with culture, a process needs to be introduced  
whereby the Christian community can practice this in familiar areas that are relatively  
easy.  
3\. Proverbs are familiar in Builsa culture; therefore, they provide a good starting place for  
learning contextualization. Proverbs are particularly potent in oral cultures since they are  
memorable, enjoyable/engaging, promote exploration and discovery, are rich with  
metaphors, fit the logic pattern of oral learners, and are windows that reveal aspects of  
the soul of the culture.

4\. Using proverbs as a model, a form of critical contextualization is introduced that helps

5\. When this same procedure is used to engage cultural issues with Scripture, the
