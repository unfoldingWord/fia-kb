While Joe is not a real person but a composite character based on missionaries  
that I interviewed and observed \(including myself\), this short narrative begins to describe  
how a new missionary could begin to use proverbs in ministry. The key is to begin as a  
learner and allow the local people to become the teachers. Tienou noted that,  
unfortunately, many missionaries come to Africa *after* their learning is finished instead of  
arriving in the middle of their learning.111 If missionaries have the attitude that they are  
still in the middle of their learning, then they will be open to new insights from the local  
culture and people. This will lead them to discover the Builsa questions. On the other  
hand, if they consider their learning to be finished, then they are more apt to give quick  
uninformed answers.  
Anthropologist Allison Howell observed many new missionaries in Ghana and  
commented that the willingness to learn is a big indicator of how thoroughly they will  
grasp the local culture and language.1V She affirmed that the use of proverbs in ministry  
falls into this category as well. If a missionary is willing to learn, then they will be open

Joe initially has a typical response to oral literature like proverbs - he overlooks  
their value in understanding the culture and the potential in theological discourse. This is  
partly due to his training in a literate culture, where the wisdom in proverbs is overlooked  
for more exact propositional truths. His theological training likely did not sensitize him to  
the value of oral literature like proverbs in oral cultures such as the Builsa, where the  
adult literacy rate is estimated at a mere 25% \(Akanling-Pare 1999\:42\). As a result, Joe  
sincerely wants to learn the Builsa culture and language but he overlooks the value of  
proverbs that are right under his feet. Once he understands the value they contain, he  
enters a new level of cultural appreciation.  
Joe also shows us how to begin using proverbs. It may help to think of oral  
literature more as an art form than a science subject. As such, the proper use of proverbs  
can be learned more like the learning of artwork as opposed to the learning of Algebra in  
school. In short, it requires careful observation, questioning, and large amounts of  
practice.  
Joe carefully observed Pastor David when he used a proverb well. By recording  
his observations for future reflection and reference, he is more likely to use them again.  
Especially note the context and occasion the proverbs are used. This forms the basis of  
the performance. Like the canvas upon which an artist paints, no proverb can be ‘painted’

Let us step back and see what Joe is learning. Joe learned theology from a very  
reputable seminary in the West. His training was excellent. By reading his English  
Scripture through his own cultural background, he was able to understand certain aspects  
of God. Christian truth, however, is greater than what one culture alone can reveal. As  
shown in Figure 7 \(Chapter 2\), there are large aspects of Christian truth that are  
overlooked by an individual culture and therefore require the engagement of mother  
tongue Scriptures \(such as Buli\) with those cultures \(such as the Builsa\) in order for other  
aspects of God to be revealed. Proverbs can be a window into the Builsa culture that  
reveal aspects of this culture to Joe. When this understanding was engaged with the Buli  
Scriptures, Joe grasped aspects of Christian truth that his previous seminary training \(as  
good as it was\) did not address.  
Tarr \(1994\) refers to this as needing two lenses on a pair of glasses in order to see  
more clearly. He observes that both Western and African cultures provide insights into

While this may sound radical to those who have not deeply engaged themselves in  
another culture, it is starting to make sense to Joe. A closer look at epistemological  
assumptions provides some insight to help us understand Joe’s discoveries. Our  
underlying assumptions of how we know what we know \(epistemology\) are often  
unquestioned; they are merely assumed. These unquestioned assumptions, however, can  
lead well-meaning and devout Christians like Joe to vastly different responses on issues  
of Scripture and culture. Hiebert \(1999\:56\) observed\:  
Many of the current debates, both in theology and in mission, seem to reflect  
epistemological differences rather than disagreements over theological content.  
The results can be disconcerting. We use the same words, but we have the uneasy  
feeling that we are not all using them in the same ways.  
Hiebert identified three commonly assumed epistemologies. These will be  
discussed in order to identify the epistemological assumptions that often underlie the  
views of Scripture and culture. These can be classified as various degrees of realism\:  
1\. A total realist position \(called Naive Realist or Positivist\) assumes that knowledge is  
totally objective, can be understood by scientific observations, and is an exact  
“picture of reality” without any distortions \(Barbour 1974\:33-35\). This position  
denies that the cultural or historical contexts affect the way people view reality since  
their view of reality is “the way it is” for all people everywhere. This epistemology  
was the underlying view for most of modem science and modem mission, often  
resulting in arrogance and ethnocentrism. Without knowing it, Joe will likely tend to

The second epistemological position is Critical Realism, which assumes that  
knowledge is both objective and subjective. There is an objective reality that can be  
observed and understood; however, our understanding of it is not complete due to  
our limitations of cultural and historical contexts. Barbour notes that valid theories  
are true and useful, but they “are abstract symbol systems which inadequately  
represent particular aspects of the world for specific purposes” \(Barbour 1974\:37\).  
“They are partial and inadequate ways of imaging what is not observable” \(Barbour  
1974\:48\). This leads to a confidence in one’s view of reality that is tempered with a  
humility that is willing to learn. Joe is beginning to enter this category. He  
understands some of the aspects of the “wings of God” from his own culture, but  
other aspects are obscure. He is limited by his own culture, and Builsa culture is  
helping him to understand this symbolism better. The critical realist view can be  
likened to the person who declares to the optometrist, “I can see well most of the  
time but I need glasses to see the blurry objects and to know what I am missing!”  
Subjective realism \(called Instrumentalist\), the third position, assumes that  
knowledge is totally subjective since it is created by culture. This view holds that one  
cannot know any exact truth since models “are neither true nor false, but only more  
or less useful mental devices” \(Barbour 1974\:36-37\). Religion is considered to be a  
“useful fiction” in that it is “a mental construct used instrumentally for particular  
purposes but not assumed to be either true or false” \(Barbour 1974\:38\). In reaction to  
modernity and the Critical Realist view above, postmodernism builds on this view of

itself provides a snapshot of how life should operate; therefore, culture dictates which

therefore, he can ignore the Builsa culture and modify what he perceives needs changing.

Before continuing, let us reflect on what transpired with Joe and Ali. They come  
from two different worldviews; hence they have different concepts of reality. Their inner  
concepts of reality have been shaped by their own cultures. Symbols, such as words, are  
used to describe this inner view of reality. For example, take the concept of God.  
According to Pierce’s triadic view of symbols, the word “GOD” \(Sign\) is used to connect  
the mental image \(Signification\) of the transcendent being to the external reality  
\(Signified\) of this transcendent being. Through the communication process, the symbols  
help to construct reality for people. As Figure 6 \(see Chapter 2\) shows, this process is not  
exact since people have different inner concepts when using symbols to discuss the same  
external reality. In this figure, we see that there is an external house that is built \(at the  
top\) and the plan sheets are used to discuss it between parties \(these are the symbols\), but  
the inner concept in each person’s mind of the way the house looks is slightly different  
from each other as well as from the external reality.  
In a cross-cultural situation, like Joe’s, this becomes even more difficult. While  
discussing the same external reality of God using the same symbol \(the word “God”\),  
they both have in their minds different inner realities. Ali sees God as far removed and  
the ultimate judge. Joe sees God as intimate and desiring to be in a close relationship with  
people.  
The constitutive model of communication reminds us that reality is continually  
constructed, reconstructed, and negotiated via the communication process. As a result,

Joe is learning these core values through these proverbs. As a result, he can then  
incorporate them into his own life and ministry to others in the Builsa area. If he does  
not, then he is likely to make serious mistakes. Before Joe, as a Western missionary,  
arrives in the Builsa land, he will have his own cultural expectations of a “Good  
Westerner” and carry these with him. Due to his own ethnocentricity, he will assume that  
the values which made him a Good Westerner will also be the same ones that will make  
him a Good Builsa. Problems occur when the values from two different cultures are  
widely different. Take for example, some American values.  
Many have written about American cultural values \(Althen 1988; Lingenfelter  
and Mayers 1986; Stewart and Bennett 1991; Storti 1999; Wanning 1991\). Nussbaum  
\(1998\) took a unique approach by analyzing over 230 American proverbs and common  
sayings in American culture. Based on the values portrayed therein, he extracted the ten  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. **1 8 9**  
most common values and called them the Ten Commandments of American culture, as  
summarized below\:  
1\. You can’t argue with success \(Be a success\).  
2\. Live and let live \(Be tolerant\)  
3\. Time flies when you’re having fun \(Have lots of fun\).  
4\. Shop till you drop  
5\. Just do it.  
6\. No pain, no gain \(Get tough. Don’t whine\).  
7\. Enough is enough \(Stand up for your rights\).  
8\. Time is money \(Don’t waste time\).  
9\. Rules are made to be broken \(Think for yourself\).  
10\. God helps those who help themselves \(work hard\).  
When comparing Nussbaum’s Ten Commandments of American culture with the  
above ten core values of Builsa culture, some major differences immediately come to the  
forefront. Compare the ten Builsa core values with the ten American commandments as  
follows\:  
1\. Instead of managing with problems since all people suffer \(Builsa value  
number one\), Americans feel that life should be fun \(American commandment number  
3\). Nussbaum \(1998\:2\) notes, “Having fun is the major preoccupation of youth, retired  
people, and many of those in between.”  
2\. Instead of knowing your role in the community \(Builsa value number 2\),  
Americans praise success above other things \(American commandment number one\) and  
they look at success as “*individualism*, freedom, goal-setting, progress, experimenting.

\[italics mine\] \(Nussbaum 1998\:1\). Nussbaum \(1998\:5\) notes, “When we Americans think  
of success, we naturally think first of individual success.”  
3\. Instead of being careful not to make mistakes to hurt others \(Builsa value  
number 3\), “Americans love freedom and privacy.. .we want to be left alone. We don’t  
want anyone interfering in our affairs, giving us advice, or trying to run our lives”  
\(Nussbaum 1998\:2\). As part of being tolerant \(American commandment number 2\),  
Americans are discouraged from interfering with other peoples’ lives.  
4\. Instead of accepting the unexplainable and unexpected as part of life \(Builsa  
value number 4\), American commandment number 7 says, “Stand up for your rights.”  
The Constitution of the U.S. says that everyone has the right to pursue happiness. If an  
unexplainable event is blocking this right, then you should stand up against it and get to  
the bottom of it. If every roof leaked in Builsa culture, they would be encouraged to  
accept it as part of life. If this same event occurred in America, it is likely that a  
government body would be expected to address it.  
5\. Both cultures agree that you need to “work hard” \(Builsa value number 5 and  
American commandment number 10\).  
6\. Instead of honoring those in authority \(Builsa value number 6\), Americans  
“... strongly dislike authority structures where people are expected to inform several other  
people before they do anything. We get an idea and we want to just do it” \(Nussbaum  
1998\:3\). As part of American commandment number 5, Americans are prone to act  
without taking time to collaborate or honor authority structures.

7\. Instead of keeping his actions and character open to those close to him \(Builsa  
value number 7\), “Americans love freedom and privacy. ..We want people to...mind their  
own business” \(Nussbaum 1998\:2\) as part of American commandment number 2.  
8\. Instead of taking the time to find the underlying meaning behind things \(Builsa  
value 8\), American commandment 8 advises, “Don’t waste time... Wasting time is as bad  
a wasting money” \(Nussbaum 1998\:3\).  
9\. According to Nussbaum, Builsa value number 9 \(use what you have today  
since tomorrow is uncertain\) does not have a parallel in the top ten American values. This  
Builsa value, however, is similar to the American proverb, “A bird in the hand is worth  
two in the bush.”  
10\. An American missionary will trust that God is chief and wants to help \(Builsa  
value 10 and American commandment 10\), even though “For most Americans, God is  
much less a concern than success, money, and time” \(Nussbaum 1998\:4\).  
While this is neither a criticism on either the Builsa or American cultures, it does  
point out that the analysis of proverbs can help identify some areas likely to bring  
tensions due to conflicting cultural value systems. Knowing this, Joe can adjust some of  
his thinking and actions in order to be seen as a “Good Builsa;” otherwise, he will  
encounter resistance and not understand why. For example, an American may think that a  
good leader is one who is an independent thinker, action-oriented, “their own person,”  
and does not waste time before implementing their plans. This type of leadership will be  
greatly misunderstood in the Builsa area. The American will likely be seen as being too  
proud since they are breaking Builsa core values No. 2, 6, and 8! Unknowingly, he is  
conveying a message that he does not intend to convey. By analyzing the Builsa proverbs

resulting in an understanding of the Builsa core values, Joe can recognize some areas that  
will create tension. In this way, he can adapt his ministry in order to reach the people.  
It should be noted here that any Christian worker who is not initially from the  
Builsa area would greatly benefit by the study of the Builsa proverbs. The example above  
was for a Western missionary; however, this equally applies to Christian workers from  
Southern Ghana, for instance, who come to work in the Builsa area. They are cross-  
cultural workers, just as Western missionaries are cross-cultural workers. Their danger in  
being misunderstood may be even greater since they may assume that since they are from  
Ghana, they understand how all Ghanaians think. They may not see the need to take the  
time to analyze the Builsa proverbs. In the end, this ministry among the Builsa people  
will be greatly hindered at best, and totally misunderstood at worst. It would be better to  
heed Abrahams \(1967\:184\) advice,  
the collection of proverbs is one of the first things one should do when entering  
into a strange community, for they can point to the sensitive spots in the social  
arrangement of the group and therefore place the observer in a position of great  
future advantage in trying to collect from and understand the members of that  
community.  
The above comparison between the Builsa values and the American  
commandments shows how a good American can be seen as a bad Builsa. Joe is learning  
to see the Builsa values through the window of their proverbs. As a result, he can adjust  
himself and begin acting as a good Builsa. This will be helpful to Joe as he becomes an  
important part of the process of contextualization in the Builsa church. Joe can provide an  
important etic perspective to help the Builsa church address “middle level” issues. This  
will be important as the church is confronted by serious issues dealing with the Builsa  
culture.

Chapter Summary  
The serious analysis of proverbs has taught Joe much about Builsa culture and  
helped him to discover aspects of Scripture that he overlooked before. Joe has learned  
many lessons that include\:  
1\. While the value of oral literature is easily overlooked by missionaries entering  
oral cultures, it is abundant and provides invaluable insight into the reasoning processes  
of oral people.  
2\. The use of oral literature, like proverbs, is more an art form than a science. To  
learn the proper use of proverbs, it requires careful observation, questioning, a lot of  
practice, and the attitude of a learner. It helps to start the learning process by learning a  
few proverbs well, and know the proper context to use them appropriately.  
3\. When outsiders learn the proper use of proverbs, their speaking is more likely  
to be regarded as adult speech. In Buluk, *biik-sobilik* or *biik-wiani* is considered the type  
of speech that carries more weight among adult speakers. The proper use of proverbs will  
often touch both emotional and ideological senses of the Builsa audience.  
4\. Since Christian truth is greater than what any one culture alone can reveal,  
there are aspects of Scripture that missionaries can learn from Builsa culture that were  
overlooked before. Builsa proverbs can be a catalyst to help reveal some of these  
discoveries. These insights can be useful in the contextualization process.  
5\. A critical realist epistemology of both Builsa culture and Scripture can help  
facilitate contextualization. This epistemology encourages missionaries to hold firmly to  
the authority of Scripture, as well as recognize that there are aspects of Scripture that they  
do not understand yet.

6\. Proverbs can be a window for missionaries to understand Builsa cultural  
values. When these are compared to the cultural values of the missionaries’ home culture,  
it can help identify areas likely to result in cultural conflict. The missionaries can then  
make adjustments in order to become good news to the people.  
7\. The analysis of proverbs can also lead to discussions and discoveries about  
hidden or deeper aspects of culture that are often overlooked. This can reveal root issues  
and “excluded middle”areas in the Builsa culture that are difficult to identify otherwise.  
For outsiders, the proverbs can serve as a window into these areas, while they can also  
serve as a mirror to Builsa as they reflect back to them aspects of Builsa culture that are  
seldom discussed openly.  
8\. Proverbs and stories are often interrelated. Each genre highlights and filters out  
aspects of Builsa culture that the other genre omits. By working together, these two  
genres complement each other well by providing a wider view of Builsa culture.  
9\. The proper use of proverbs can help missionaries in evangelism, discipleship,  
and counseling, as well as contextualization. The narrative provides examples of each.  
10\. Missionaries provide an etic perspective that is helpful in the contextualization  
process. The analysis of proverbs can help sharpen the missionaries’ insight and be a  
catalyst for contextualization.
