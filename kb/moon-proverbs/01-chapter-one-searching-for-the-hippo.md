A young girl once longed to see a hippopotamus in the river near her house  
\(Clapp 1996\:188\). One day, her parents informed her that a hippo was in the river and  
that she should hurry and see it. Excited, she ran to the riverbank looking for the hippo.  
Her enthusiasm quickly turned to disappointment - she could not find it. She searched up  
and down the bank but the hippo was nowhere to be seen. Finally, she scrambled on top  
of a big rock in order to get a good vantage point from which to view the entire river.  
Still, she could not see the hippo. Disappointed, she went home and told her parents how  
hard she looked but could not find the hippo. “You didn’t know it,” explained her  
parents, “but the very rock that you climbed on was the hippo. It was under your feet and  
you didn’t know it!” Often, the very things we search for are “under our feet.”  
Background of the Problem  
Many writers have observed Christianity’s lack of contextualization in Africa  
\(Bediako 2001a; Bevans 2002; Hiebert et al. 1999; Schreiter 1985; Turaki 2000;  
Whiteman 1997\). While the church in Africa is growing at a tremendous rate, the  
Scripture has not reached deeply into the worldview of the culture. Events like the 1994  
genocide in Rwanda, considered by many as one of the most Christian countries in Africa  
and the center of the famous East Africa revival, reveal the tragic consequences when the  
Scripture reaches only the surface level of culture but does not penetrate into the deeper

deal adequately with everyday concerns, let alone issues of life and death. Missionaries  
and national church leaders are searching up and down the river for tools and insights that  
will facilitate an engagement of the Scripture and culture. So far, there has largely been

Even the existing theological training has not often yielded the desired fruits of  
contextualization. During the 2001 all-Africa conference of the African Theological  
Fellowship held in Cote D’Ivoire, various members said that “the kind of theological  
formation that Africans receive in existing institutions, using existing patterns of training,  
does not equip for meeting the real needs and problems of life” \(Bediako 2001a\:32\). A  
Ghanaian pastor lamented to me, “After all of my training \[in Bible College\] was  
finished, I went back to the village and found that it did not fit!”1 His training did not  
address the cultural issues that he was facing in practical ministry in Ghana. Here is the  
mystery\: why has so much lip service been given to contextualization in Africa, yet there  
is still so little impact on the training of African pastors right up to today? Whiteman  
\(1997\:6\) observed this lack of practical contextualization and concluded, “I believe the  
only way through this maze is to discover the tools and perspectives of contextualization  
and then have the courage to implement them.” This research seeks to discover and  
experiment with one of those little-used tools - traditional proverbs.  
Our basic assumption is that, for a deep engagement of the culture and Scripture  
to occur, a closer look at cultural symbols is needed. Indigenous symbols can open  
windows that reveal aspects of the culture that are otherwise hidden. Local proverbs are  
an abundant and useful source of symbols that are often overlooked. Mbiti \(1969\:86\)  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. noted, “Proverbs are common ways of expressing religious ideas and feelings.” Proverbs,  
then, may serve as open windows into the worldview of the culture, thereby providing a  
good place to start in understanding the deeper aspects of culture. As this understanding  
of the culture is engaged with the Scripture in a hermeneutical community, critical  
contextualization can occur \(Hiebert 1987\). Proverbs may be one of the tools that God  
has placed in African cultures to facilitate contextualization - and they are right under  
our feet!  
African literary great, Chinua Achebe \(1959\:7\), noted, “Proverbs are the palm oil  
with which words are eaten.” While learning the Buli language of the Builsa people in  
Northern Ghana, I personally began to appreciate this oral art form as a powerful vehicle  
to communicate commonly held truth. This began a process where I started to collect  
Builsa proverbs, experiment with them in ministry, and reflect with other church leaders  
on how these proverbs could be useful in ministry among the Builsa people. I began to  
understand the potential of Mbiti’s \(1995\:55\) claim, “Proverbs...form a bridge between  
traditional African religiosity and biblical teaching.” I wondered how far this bridge  
would take us if we took proverbs seriously in discipling and theological training. Could  
we push this even further and change the metaphor from a bridge to a well? Would  
proverbs allow us to dig through the surface level of culture and help us reach down into  
the deeper, hidden worldview level? Would this well tap into a good source that would  
facilitate the engagement of Builsa culture with Scripture that would result in  
contextualization? Would it save some lives or even some entire societies from the  
disasters they fall into when their Christian understanding is superficial to their culture?

Statement of the Problem  
While “Split-Level” Christianity is functioning in Builsa culture, large areas of  
the culture are not addressed by Scripture. The contextualization of Christianity in Builsa  
culture is then limited. How can this be corrected? What tools or perspectives can be  
found that will point the way forward for contextualization? What are the roles of  
missionaries and national church leaders/lay people in this process? The purpose of this  
research is to discover and describe how Builsa proverbs can be used in a hermeneutical  
community to help begin the process of contextualizing Christianity in Builsa culture.  
The focus is on a process that can be used to promote contextualization rather than a  
finished product.  
While the focus of this research is contextualization, several steps along the way  
are needed in order to get to the final goal.  
First, proverbs open an understanding of the traditional Builsa culture and religion  
that may otherwise be obscured. In order to determine how the analysis of proverbs by  
cultural insiders \(emic perspective\) may help them to understand and appreciate their  
own culture and Scripture, the first research question is posed, “What reflections are  
Builsa church leaders expressing of their own culture and Scripture as they focus on their  
proverbs?”  
Second, proverbs can be useful for those outside the culture looking in \(etic  
perspective\), as well as for those inside the culture. In order to determine how the  
analysis of proverbs by cross-cultural workers may help them understand another culture  
and Scripture, the second research question is posed, “What reflections are missionaries  
expressing of local culture and Scripture as they focus on proverbs?”  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. Third, a hermeneutical community can facilitate contextualization. To discover  
what process can be used to encourage the collection/study/analysis of proverbs, and the  
engagement of Scripture within a hermeneutical community via the use of proverbs, the  
third research question is posed, “What encourages the formation and on-going operation  
of a hermeneutical community and their engagement of Scripture with proverbs?”  
Fourth, when the Scripture and Builsa culture are engaged via proverbs, the  
resulting expression of Christianity will need to be communicated within Builsa culture.  
To observe evidence of contextualization as a result of focusing on proverbs, the fourth  
research question is posed, “How do Builsa respond as proverbs are used to communicate  
Christianity?”  
The above research questions helped to focus and guide the research. The  
following delimitations also help to sharpen the research focus.  
Delimitations and Limitations  
Folklore or oral literature for Africans was identified early as “a key to their ideas  
and a standard of their powers of thought” \(Theal 1886\[1970\]\:v\). The vast body of oral  
literature is an abundant source of cultural material; however, this study will only  
investigate the local genre of *yam wamgsima,* which can be glossed as proverbs. While  
proverbs, in themselves, do not provide the entire mosaic of a culture, they are one  
critical piece that is often neglected. This study will exemplify what can be done with  
other areas of oral literature.  
While oral literature, like proverbs, reflect certain aspects of culture, this study  
recognizes the limitations of language in communication. E.T. Hall \(1959\:45\) notes that

humans use ten “Primary Message Systems” to communicate and only one of these  
involves language. As a result, proverbs can reveal significant aspects of culture, but  
much is also revealed by non-linguistic forms of communication.  
Hesselgrave \(1991\:327\) observed the abundance of proverbs across Africa\:  
“Thousands of proverbs and aphorisms form an indispensable part of daily  
communication throughout the cities and villages of Africa and other tribal areas.” For  
the purposes of this research, I will focus on the Builsa people in Northern Ghana, with  
supplementary material from other people groups in Northern Ghana and from African  
students studying at Asbury Theological Seminary.  
Honeck noted that there are several ways to view proverbs \(depending on the  
primary disciplines involved\), and this affects how the particular research is conducted.  
Honeck \(1997\:5\) concludes, “There is no overarching theory of proverbs. Instead,  
different views have emerged.” He describes seven major views \(see Table 1 on the  
following page\).  
The cognitive view regards proverbs as a “theoretical mental construct, and  
emphasizes the universal microcognitive, pancultural processes that underlie them”  
\(Honeck 1997\:43\). The emphasis is on finding what universal mental processes humans  
use to learn, understand, and apply proverbs. The proverbs are extracted from their  
cultural context in order to minimize cultural differences and discover universal  
principles. In contrast to the cognitive view, the cultural view considers proverbs to be  
“cultural linguistic products, created and used in social situations for social purposes...  
\[that\] largely entail the codification of important lessons in the culture” \(Honeck  
1997\:31\). Instead of minimizing the cultural context \(as in the cognitive view\), this is

they are used. This also includes the study of how proverbs originate, themes of proverb

In the formal view, the focus is to “describe the linguistic and semantic-logic  
properties of proverbs” \(Honeck 1997\:11\). Proverbs are regarded as a unique language  
form that can be defined, classified, and analyzed resulting in taxonomies of various  
proverbs collections and characteristics that define proverbs. The literary view analyzes  
how proverbs are used in various genres \(e.g., prose, poetry, song\) by various authors  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. 8  
through time \(e.g., Shakespeare, Dickinson, Achebe\). This view offers “an intriguing  
look at authors, their concerns and their times” \(Honeck 1997\:28\). The personal view of  
proverbs may also be called the phenomenological view. This is a more subjective study  
by people that have personal interests in proverbs and study them for various reasons  
\(e.g., personal use in conversation, attraction to proverbs, etc.\). Basically, this is “an  
egocentric and non-scientific view” \(Honeck 1997\:6\).  
The practical view recognizes that proverbs have pragmatic functions that  
“facilitate memory, teach, and persuade..Their practical uses draw on these same  
functions” \(Honeck 1997\:29\). Honeck lists several practical uses of proverbs to include\:  
treatment of substance abuse, psychotherapy, test, mental status, teach children abstract  
thinking, asses attitudes toward work and life, determine competency to stand trial,  
advertising, and intelligence testing. Finally, the religious view regards proverbs as  
expressions of religious wisdom that reflect how people should live their lives with each  
other. Religious texts \(e.g., Scripture\) are studied to understand the intended meaning or  
“how they fitted into the larger theological realm and helped to spread a religious  
message” \(Honeck 1997\:26\). Some researchers analyze the form and message of  
proverbs in wisdom literature, while others have compared proverbs between various  
religious traditions.  
This study is limited to the cultural and religious views. In these disciplines, I  
limited the research to explore how proverbs function as windows to understand Builsa  
culture, religion, and personal ethics, and how this helps to contextualize Christianity  
within that culture. The cultural view is helpful since it regards proverbs as oral literature  
that arises from a particular culture and functions as an integral part of that culture. The

Another delimitation is that biblical proverbs were not prominent in this research.  
This may seem odd at first glance since proverbs were also an important genre in biblical  
times. Not only does the Old Testament contain an entire book devoted to proverbs, but  
the genre of proverbs is included in the New Testament as well. In addition, proverbs in  
Scripture often function to encourage reflection on practical living and to reinforce moral  
precepts. While it may seem logical that compatible genres would lead to fruitful  
discussions for contextualization, biblical proverbs are not featured in this research for  
the following reasons\:  
1\. The biblical book of Proverbs has not yet been translated into the Buli  
language. Consequently, when the Builsa people engage Scripture, they do not yet have  
ready access to the book of Proverbs.  
2\. When Builsa hear a *yam wamagsima* spoken, they immediately recognize it as  
such. While *yam wamagsima* can be roughly glossed as the English word “proverb,” non-  
indigenous proverbs, including biblical proverbs, do not affect a Builsa audience the  
same way. Y*am wamagsima* are unique expressions of Builsa culture that often invoke  
strong emotional responses among the Builsa; whereas, biblical \(and other\) proverbs do  
not typically create the same response. So, *yam wamagsima* are a distinct genre within  
the broader genre of proverbs and similar figures of speech. This will be discussed further  
in the next section and exemplified in subsequent chapters.  
3\. In the field research, I interjected some biblical proverbs to see how this may  
help the contextualization process. This did not seem to be particularly helpful, perhaps

Definitions  
The following definitions will clarify the discussion\:  
Oral Literature. Two East African scholars, Nandwas and Bukenya \(1983\:1\)  
defined oral literature as “those utterances, whether spoken, recited or sung, whose  
composition and performance exhibit to an appreciable degree the artistic characteristics  
of accurate observation, vivid imagination and ingenious expression.” Put more simply,  
Okpewho \(1992\:3-4\) states, “Oral literature ... simply means literature delivered by  
words of mouth”, and this represents the verbal aspect of folklore “which comprises  
riddles, puns, tongue-twisters, proverbs, recitations, chants, songs and stories.” Finnegan  
\(1970\) adds to this list drama, poems, dance, myths, and drum language. While some  
object to the use of this term since it ignores the distinctions between orality and literacy  
\(Ong 1982\:12\), others prefer the terms “oral literature” \(Finnegan 1970\), “oral tradition”  
\(Baldick 1990\:156\), or “orature” \(Thiong'o 1986\) over the formerly used term “folklore”  
due to the negative connotations often given by Europeans associated with the term  
“folklore.” Proverbs are a part of, and intimately connected to, the larger body of oral  
literature. Proverbs are often told in the context of stories or songs, either as the  
summation of the point or as an introduction to the performance. Like other oral  
literature, proverbs provide an aesthetic expression as an oral art form; therefore, the  
performance of the proverb carries the meaning, not just the words or logic of the  
utterance.  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. Proverbs. Scholars have strived for years to provide an inclusive definition of  
proverbs. Wolfgang Meider \(Meider and Holmes 2000\:37\), a leading contemporary  
paremiologist, considered Archer Taylor’s classic text, *The Proverb* to be the “Bible of  
proverb scholarship.” Taylor \(1962 \[1931\]\:37\) concluded, “The definition of a proverb is  
too difficult to repay the undertaking... An incommunicable quality tells us this sentence  
is proverbial and one that is not.” In other words, a proverb is easily identified when  
spoken but hard to define. Shirley Arora \(1984\) notes that certain characteristics of  
proverbs often include traditionality, currency, repetition, certain grammatical or  
syntactical features, metaphor, semantic markers \(parallelism, paradox, irony, etc.\),  
lexical markers \(archaic words, etc.\), and phonic markers \(rhyme, meter, alliteration,  
etc.\). The more these are included in the phrase or saying, the more likely it is to be  
regarded as a proverb. Nonetheless, several have attempted to provide a working  
definition of a proverb. A proverb has been defined as “ ... a short, pithy saying that  
encodes the philosophical outlook, religious concepts and world-view of African society  
in a digestible form” \(Healey and Sybertz 2000\[1996\]\:34\). While proverbs may not  
portray the entire philosophical outlook, religious concepts and world-view, they most  
certainly reveal important aspects. Lord John Russell \(1792-1878\) offered the following  
definition, “A proverb is the wit of one, and the wisdom of many” \(Meider and Holmes  
2000\:96\).

*Biik-pieli* is used to speak very clearly and simply. This type of speech is used by  
a child or someone speaking to a child. An example is the proverb, **“Ba kan dak yauk a**  
**sak biiga”** \(They do not point to the elephant and show the child\).  
*Biik-wiani* is used to speak in a way that obscures the meaning so that some of the  
listeners will not understand the meaning \(even though they may understand every  
individual word spoken\). This can be used when adults do not want children to  
understand what is being discussed or to insult another person. An example is the  
proverb, *“Fi dan sak bulorik dueni deka, ku chum le faari fi ma*” **\(If you teach an ugly**

type of *biik* indicates that you understand the Builsa people and their culture. This

Builsa. The Builsa people \(also written in various literature as Bulsa, Bulisa, and  
Kanjargas\) comprise approximately 100,000 \(Kroger 2001\:30\) and predominately live in  
the Upper East Region of Ghana, West Africa, while many have also migrated to  
Southern Ghana and beyond. Their language is called Buli and their land/society is  
referred to as Buluk \(Akanling-Pare 1999\:38\). Buli is in the Gur language family under  
the Oti-Volta subgrouping, being most closely related to the Konni languge \(Naden  
1988\:17-18\). The Builsa are largely subsistence farmers living in scattered villages with  
the largest towns being Sandema \(in the North\) and Fumbisi \(in the South\). According to

Culture. Culture is easier to observe by comparing various cultures than it is to  
define. There is no universally accepted definition of culture, though this concept was  
first introduced into the English language in 1871 by Edward B. Tylor \(Langness 1987  
\[1974\]\: 1\). Whiteman \(2001\) defines culture as “That complex array of ideas in a person’s  
mind which are expressed in the form of material artifacts and observable behavior.”  
Rynkiewich \(2003\) states that culture is, “A more or less integrated system of learned,  
shared and contested ways of thinking, valuing, and feeling that serves to adapt a people  
to their changing environment and provides a foundation for change.” From an  
anthropological perspective, the emphasis is on a holistic plan or blueprint for living  
carried in the minds of people that is learned and always adjusting to meet the needs and  
questions of people. The norms and values established guide the interactions between  
people, including communication patterns. These norms for communicating are “basic for  
contextualizing the Gospel and for constructing meaningful local theologies” \(Luzbetak  
1988\:157\). It is also important to note that culture is a set of learned ideas that are  
transmitted by a set of symbols that have meanings understood by the group. When

Builsa culture and religion. African religion has been described as “a way of life  
or life itself, where a distinction or separation is not made between religion and other  
areas of human existence” \(Magesa 1997\:25\). As a result of this close union, Dickson  
\(1984\:29\) noted, “African culture and religion are bound up together, so much so that the  
term culture...\[is\] an umbrella description which subsumes religion.” This close union of  
religion and culture is noteworthy since it indicates the complexity of understanding  
Builsa religion, as well as showing that tools for understanding the religion will be  
located in the culture \(e.g., proverbs\).  
Soul of Culture. Luzbetak identifies three levels whereby societies have organized  
their culture. The surface layer reveals the “meaningless forms, the ‘shapes,’ the symbols  
minus their meaning” \(Luzbetak 1988\:223\). In order to uncover the meaning behind these  
forms, the second layer of culture must be uncovered. The second layer is the inner  
function or system of meaning behind the forms. The third or deepest level is the basic  
psychology or soul of the culture \(1988\:223\). Valuable clues to understanding this level

will be found in myths, rituals, folk tales, proverbs, etc. \(Luzbetak 1988\:251\). Luzbetak  
\(1988\:249-250\) describes this third level further,  
The unifying psychological tendency has been called by various names.. .genius,  
philosophy of life, mentality, psychology, inner logic, and Geist. Sumner spoke of  
ethos, while Sapir preferred the terms style and patterning. Benedict spoke of the  
main spring and patterns of culture.. .meant are the underlying notions, values,  
and motives; meant are the starting points of reasoning, reacting, and acting; and  
meant are the basic premises, emotionally charged attitudes, and goals. Such an  
underlying and basic set of starting points in reasoning, reacting, and acting tends  
to be consistent in itself and is so basic that it permeates the whole culture and  
gives a certain oneness. This is what is commonly referred to as a people’s  
“mentality,” “psychology,” or “soul.” It is what we call the third or deep level of  
culture.  
It is this third level of culture that is most difficult to identify and understand, even for  
those that are immersed in it and call it their own. This is the level that must be  
understood, however, in order for the critical engagement of the culture and Scripture to  
occur.  
Sign and Symbol. A sign has been defined as “as word, object, action, event,  
pattern, quality, relation, person, or concrete particular that serves as a vehicle for a  
conception of some perceived reality” \(Hiebert et al. 1999\:232\). In short, it is “something  
present that stands for something absent” \(Leeds-Hurwitz 1993\:6\) or something that  
points to something else. Signs help to “connect the world of exterior realities to inner  
mental worlds” \(Hiebert et al. 1999\:232\). A symbol is a subset of a sign. Peirce  
\(1955\[1940J\) called symbols unmotivated or arbitrary signs, meaning that they do not  
necessarily resemble the reality that they represent \(i.e., they are not obviously related\).  
Symbols are the basic building blocks that societies use to construct the mental worlds of  
reality in order to give order and meaning \(Hiebert et al. 1999\:234\). Burke \(1968\:3-24\)

points out that humans not only use symbols, but they make them and misuse them. This  
implies that they are continually being transformed and recreated.  
Engagement of Scripture and Culture. More than just Western classical theology  
with African clothing, the engagement of Scripture and culture reveals both the image of  
God and the stain of sin in culture. Similarly, both the universal continuity and cultural  
applications of Scripture are revealed as the mother tongue Scripture is employed. Since  
this concept is essential to the research, it deserves further explanation, as given by  
African theologian, Kwame Bediako \(2001b\:2\),  
The engagement of gospel and culture means more than the altering of surface  
elements like music, dance and artifacts. It is also more than “spiritual salvation”,  
more than the saving of the spiritual part of us. It is also not about the  
demonisation of our culture. Rather, gospel and cultural engagement is about  
the conversion of cultures, the turning to Christ and turning over to Christ of all  
that is there in us, about us and round about us that has defined and shaped us  
when Jesus meets us, so that the elements of our cultural identity are brought  
within the orbit of discipleship.  
Contextual theology results as the mother tongue Scripture and the culture are  
fully engaged, thereby creating dialogue between the two. Both the Scripture and culture  
are more fully understood and appreciated in this process.  
Critical Contextualization. A process whereby the Scripture and culture are fully  
engaged has been called critical contextualization \(Hiebert 1987\). Hiebert et al \(1999\:22\)  
describe this as a four-step process that includes phenomenological analysis, ontological  
reflection, critical evaluation, and missiological transformation. Uncritical acceptance  
and total rejection of cultural beliefs and practices are two inappropriate responses to the  
interaction of Scripture and culture. Critical Contextualization is an attempt to bridge  
these two responses and provide a response to cultural issues that is Scripturally faithful  
as well as culturally relevant. Whiteman \(1997\:2\) notes, “Essentially, contextualization is

concerned with how the Gospel and culture relate to one another across geographic space  
and down through time.” Contextualization includes the proper use of indigenous forms  
in the life of the church, the developing of contextual theology and the proper addressing  
of local needs by the church in order to transform society \(Gilliland 1989\:27\).  
Hermeneutical Community. Critical contextualization is best done by a  
community of Christians in dialogue to both initiate and affirm it. Gilliland \(1989\:317\)  
notes, “the goal of the critical method is to arrive at contextualized practices which have  
the consensus of the redeemed community .” When discussing the process of constructing  
local theologies, Schreiter describes the “community as the theologian.” He \(1985\:16\)  
states, “The community itself \[is\] the prime author of theology in local contexts. The  
Holy Spirit, working in and around the believing community, gives shape and expression  
to Christian experience.” While the critical role of the hermeneutical community has been  
widely affirmed, little has been written to discuss how this community is formed, how it  
functions, is led and motivated. When discussing indigenous theologies, Tienou  
\(1993\:249\) states, “How to get the faith community, the so-called grass-roots Christians,  
to be effective participants in theological decision-making still remains a challenge for  
the professional theologian today, and perhaps tomorrow.” While this community of  
believers locally and beyond is needed to properly engage the Scripture and culture, the  
process is not equally clear. Even though the church is a hermeneutical community and  
contextualization is done to a certain degree in the life of the church through the songs,  
prayers, testimonies, sermons, rituals, etc., this research aims to see how church leaders  
can more intentionally engage “excluded middle” issues.

Primal Religion. While Hiebert et al \(1999\:75\) use the term “folk religion” to  
refer to “the religious beliefs and practices of the common folk,” Turner \(1977\) rejects  
this designation and suggests the use of the term “primal religion.” Gillian Bediako  
\(2000\:12\) agrees with Turner that the term “primal” is “the best available to describe the  
varied, yet common religious traditions around the world from which the majority of the  
world’s Christians have come.” She adds that primal does not mean primitive. Walls  
\(1996\:121\) also affirms Turner’s preference for the term “primal” since it “helpfully  
underlines two features of the religions of the people indicated\: their historical anteriority  
and their basic, elemental status in human experience.” The term primal then goes  
beyond the term folk, since the use of the term primal religion acknowledges that this is

My theological assumption is that God has revealed aspects of Himself to all  
cultures and people \(Romans 1, John 1\:9\). God has given to each person \(regardless of  
cultural or geographical location\) some light that testifies of Him. This grace is  
ministered to each person by the activity of the Holy Spirit, and it is meant to draw  
people to a new relationship with God through Jesus Christ. John Wesley describes this  
as the prevenient grace of God.  
Richardson \(1981\) has popularized the notion that God has placed a witness of  
Himself within each culture. He demonstrates how God’s general revelation \(which he  
calls the Melchizedek factor\) often prepares the way for people to understand and receive  
God’s special revelation \(the Abraham factor\). The Creator God has provided redemptive  
analogies within the culture that Christian witnesses can use to help explain the Good  
News of Jesus Christ. He also calls this “redemptive lore” since it “contributes to the  
redemption of a people solely by facilitating their understanding of what redemption  
means” \(1981\:59\). I regard some of the Builsa proverbs as “redemptive lore” that are  
evidence of the prevenient grace of God working within the Builsa culture.  
At the same time, the fall of Adam and Eve in Genesis introduced sin into the  
world. Consequently, all people and resulting cultures contain evidence of sin \(Romans  
3\:23\). Some of the Builsa proverbs also reflect the sinfulness of humankind. While  
missionaries have often worked to identify the sins in a host culture, my assumption is

aspects of culture are marred by sin and which are evidence of God’s prevenient grace, as  
they engage culture with Scripture and use the mother tongue Scripture as the interpreter  
of their culture and tradition. These assumptions undergird this research.  
So, when looking for perspectives and tools for contextualization, it may be best  
to look for evidence of God’s prevenient grace already active in Builsa culture. Perhaps  
some proverbs are huge rocks placed by God within Builsa culture - and they are right  
under our feet. They can be used in a hermeneutical community as a bridge to  
contextualize Christianity in Builsa culture. To understand how these rocks may help us  
to find the hippo of contextualization, a review of the relevant literature is first needed.
