The use of proverbs again attracts other people groups into the circle. Note how  
those from other people groups quickly joined the discussions, finally resulting in a larger  
group in Tamale. The same occurred at the Asbury Theological Seminary in Wilmore,  
KY. During classes on “Contextualization” and “The Indigenous Church,” several  
African students and I formed an African Proverbs discussion group that met over a ten  
week period. In the midst of busy study schedules, these students took the time to analyze  
proverbs and interpret them in light of Scripture, resulting in contextual theology. This  
interest indicates the potential impact of the church beginning a process to engage  
Scripture with culture via proverbs in different contexts.  
This narrative also shows how the circle could be widened to include various  
venues as well as contexts. While we have already seen the church leaders meeting in the  
local church, we now see some of the possibilities with theological training schools. Up  
to the present, I have largely focused on grass-roots theologizing and not formal  
theological schools. While much could be said here, suffice it to say that the intentional  
use of proverbs in theological training schools could promote a discovery of culture and  
engagement with Scripture that could promote contextualization at the formal school  
level. Instead of replacing formal theological training schools altogether with oral  
teaching methods, the narrative shows how oral teaching methods can supplement formal  
theological training. By mixing oral education with formal schooling and perhaps some  
apprenticeship/mentoring, a better process for training primary oral learners may be  
discovered. This is mentioned in passing since the focus of the research is on using  
proverbs at the grass-roots level among the Builsa who are primarily oral learners.

At the grass-roots level, we have mentioned the importance of the hermeneutical  
community. The narrative reveals some important details regarding the starting and  
functioning of the hermeneutical community.  
First of all, we again see the important role of the catalyst in this process. There  
must be someone like Joe and/or Immanuel who see the potential. The catalyst\(s\) are  
instrumental in bringing forth the idea, starting to gather people together, and arranging  
the transportation/lodging/food if necessary. This could be a missionary like Joe or a  
church leader like Immanuel, but it is more effective when both combine their efforts.  
In order for the discussions to continue over a longer period of time, it will be  
necessary for a local church leader to be committed to the catalyst/organizer role. Once  
the missionary goes home, the local church leaders will need to maintain the discussions;  
otherwise, it will likely slip away. When I left Buluk to come home, the church leaders  
met for a period of time to discuss proverbs, but they eventually stopped meeting. On the  
other hand, the proverbs meetings in Tamale continued when the missionary went home,  
since a church leader in Tamale continued to play the role of catalyst/organizer.  
Another important, but easily overlooked, aspect of the discussion meetings is the  
provision of food. A Builsa church leader explained to me that he wanted to keep the  
discussion meetings alive when I was away but “If I call them to meet, they may think  
that I will have prepared food to eat. Since the money is not there, we can’t eat and  
meet.” Since the Builsa are all subsistence farmers, it is difficult to miss a day of farming.  
The provision of food at the meeting conclusion is most helpful. Hence the proverb,  
***“Popo jiib alaa jii ngmazuk jiib*” \(The stomach load carries the head load\).**

The catalyst/organizer helps in many other ways as well. They can keep notes of  
the discussions for future use, they help start the discussion and steer the conversation  
back on track, make suggestions for application \(e.g., encourage the group to more  
intentionally use proverbs in ministry each week and report on the results\), and make  
suggestions for future events \(such as the larger meeting in Tamale\).  
It is interesting to note how informal “assignments” were helpful in encouraging  
more thoughtful reflection. By asking the church leaders to use proverbs in ministry each  
week and then report back, this encouraged them to reflect on proverbs throughout the  
week. One church leader noted that, once he started thinking this way, proverbs just kept  
coming to him all the time. The more he thought of proverbs, the more he used them, and  
vice versa. The assignment also urged many of them to write down some of these  
reflections so that they could discuss them at the next meeting. This again promoted  
reflection and discovery that would make for richer discussions with the other church  
leaders. Some other “assignments” that were used included asking the church leaders to  
find proverbs that related to the GR picture books, and sitting with community elders to  
collect additional proverbs. These all helped to provided more material for reflection and  
use.

Let us take a quick break and discuss what we see occurring here. For the first  
time in the narrative, we hear from some of the youth. The Builsa youth are plentiful in  
many of the churches. They are often quite active as well. Youth ministry can have a  
large impact in the future direction of the Builsa church. The use of proverbs to engage  
Scripture among youth can affirm aspects of Builsa culture, while using Scripture to  
modify or reject other aspects. This process is critical for contextualization; otherwise,  
youth will likely fall into the two knee-jerk reactions that we have seen of full acceptance  
or full rejection without an engagement of the issues.  
Since proverbs contain open-ended metaphors, they can reveal issues relating to  
youth as well as adults. Notice how the proverbs promote reflections about pressing  
youth issues - teen sexual activity and pregnancy, difficulties in following God’s path,  
fear of witchcraft, etc. These are brought to the surface and openly discussed instead of  
being pushed into the “excluded middle.” When both Scripture and proverbs speak about  
these issues, then this carries a lot of weight and youth may take this more seriously.  
Due to the forces of globalization and westernization, some are concerned that  
local proverbs will be discarded by the youth as archaic and not helpful for the  
contemporary context. The narrative here shows that the Builsa youth are still interested

There are several other contexts where I observed proverbs being used effectively  
in the church that I will just mention in passing. When a large crowd is gathered \(e.g., in  
public ceremonies such as baby dedications, well dedications, weddings, funerals, etc.\),  
proverbs can help communicate the message in a way that both touches the emotions and  
the ideology. When Builsa leave the ceremony, they are likely to remember the proverb  
amidst everything else that transpired. Another context that was effective was when a  
Builsa pastor called the local house heads together in order to hear proverbs from them  
and then discuss them. This turned into a wonderful evangelistic opportunity, as the  
discussion on proverbs gradually revealed cultural issues like witchcraft, drinking, and  
polygamy that the nonbelievers want to know about. In the end, the house head said,  
“Our ways are changing. Our people did not know in the past. Now, Christians should  
teach us well.” These are just a few of the ways that can be used with proverbs in  
ministry.  
Summary and Application  
The above narrative demonstrates some of the possible ways that proverbs can be  
useful in the Builsa area for evangelism, discipleship, and contextualization. Several  
methods were used in various contexts and venues. I have described just a few of the  
possibilities. By analyzing and comparing this and all of the preceding narratives, the  
following summarizes a helpful process that can promote contextualization in the Builsa  
church.  
1\. A catalyst is helpful to call together the hermeneutical community. Ideally, this  
community should be represented by both emic and etic voices, where possible. The  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. **2 4 1**  
catalyst’s role is to help people see the potential of oral literature when it is engaged with  
Scripture. This person or persons will also start to gather people and consider how to  
work out the logistics of transportation, food, and lodging, when needed.  
2\. Participants should be prepared to discuss a proverb with the group. They  
should state the proverb and then explain its meaning/origin in the culture. They should  
explain the context that this proverb is often used, as well as explain its meaning with  
other oral literature when needed \(e.g., a story or song may be needed to explain the  
proverb more fully\). The other participants should also contribute to this exegesis. The  
aim is to dig down to the roots of the proverb to see the cultural values that this proverb  
reveals. In order to reveal these roots, some questions to ask are\:  
a. Who says this proverb and to whom do they say it \(e.g., elder to young, elder to  
elder, chief to disputing parties, etc.\)?  
b. When do they say this? What is done before or after this is spoken?  
c. Where do they say this \(e.g., market, ceremonies, private insult\)?  
d. Why do they use it \(e.g., complement, insult, clarify meaning, entertain, etc.\)?  
3\. The participants search the Scripture for appropriate Scripture portions or  
themes that address the issues in the proverb. While this can be done in a cursory way  
\(such as the youth meeting\), Scripture should be identified that speaks to the deeper roots  
identified above. Scripture can speak most powerfully at this level. This helps  
participants to identify which roots the Scripture will affirm, modify, or reject. The aim is  
to encourage critical reflection so that participants do not fall into the typical responses of  
rejecting everything or accepting everything. Reliance upon the Holy Spirit to guide this  
process is critical. Discussions at this level are most fruitful for contextualization.

5\. Encourage the group to come to a consensus on suggestions for ministry. This  
is where the pieces are pulled together and the group seeks to make application for their  
own lives and ministry. At this point, the group should think specifically and ask  
questions like\:  
a. How can the results of this analysis be used in teaching, evangelism,  
counseling, and discipling?  
b. What is the best way to remember and communicate this? This will be helpful  
for transforming not only our own lives but also the life of the church.

d. In addition to the proverb, what other oral literature, object lessons, metaphors,  
pictures \(e.g., GR picture books\), etc. would be helpful in communicating this

6\. It is helpful for someone in the group to record the discussion for future use.  
This can be done on cassette tape \(for future transcription\) or handwritten \(for future  
typing\). By recording the discussion, others can review and use it later when other issues  
arise. It becomes a part of church history that may help others to deal with similar issues.  
In this regard, it is best to write the results in the mother tongue. This maintains the  
integrity of the thinking processes and conceptual categories used. Then, the results can  
be back-translated into a lingua franca \(such as English\) for wider discussions with other  
people groups. This is more laborious than simply writing everything in a lingua franca  
but the mother tongue usage encourages creative indigenous expressions to truly emerge  
in the contextualization process \(Sanneh 1989\:207\).  
7\. Once this process is familiar with “easily understood” proverbs, do the same  
process with more difficult proverbs. This will stretch the participants to understand and  
apply the process more fully. After these difficult proverbs are addressed, do the same  
process with cultural issues that the church is encountering, in the same way that  
proverbs were engaged with Scripture.

various ways. Adaptations can be made for dealing with youth, the elderly, and various  
officials. In addition, various venues can be used, such as the local church, theological  
schools, regional training centers \(e.g., TICCS\), etc. This can produce contextual  
expressions of Christianity to be used for evangelism, counseling, and discipleship. A
