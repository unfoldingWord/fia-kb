# Using Proverbs to Contextualize Christianity in the Builsa Culture  
# of Ghana, West Africa  
The purpose of this research is to discover how Builsa proverbs can help a  
hermeneutical community begin the process of contextualizing Christianity in Builsa  
culture. A process to promote contextualization is emphasized rather than a finished  
product. A narrative format demonstrates how proverbs facilitate contextualization as  
Scripture engages culture.  
Data was collected using participant-observation, group discussions, and intensive  
interviews from 1992 - 2004 in Ghana and the U.S. Composite characters embodying  
typical responses of the group are presented in the narrative in order to describe the  
contextualization process.  
Both the emic and etic perspectives were employed. In the emic perspective, the  
Builsa church leaders in the Bible Church of Africa were better equipped to engage  
cultural issues with Scripture after they first learned to engage root values in their own  
proverbs with Scripture. After digging to the roots of Builsa proverbs, the Builsa  
reflected upon aspects of their own primal religion and culture that were often neglected  
in church. Proverbs became a mirror to reflect back some of their own hidden cultural  
values. These roots were then engaged with Scripture in order to affirm, modify, or reject  
aspects of the culture. This process was then applied to cultural issues in the church  
resulting in contextualized expressions of Christianity.  
From the etic perspective, proverbs were a window into the soul of the Builsa  
culture revealing aspects often hidden to outsiders. When this understanding was engaged

proverbs as oral art, the outsiders’ message was better appreciated and communicated.  
When comparing the values contained in Builsa proverbs to American values, differences  
were highlighted indicating potential areas of cultural misunderstanding.  
A contextualization process is described that incorporates the use of symbols,  
various genres, mother tongue Scripture, a widening hermeneutical community, the Holy  
Spirit, and an understanding of epistemology. Conclusions for the missionary community  
include\: appreciating oral literature in primary oral cultures, finding proper roles in the  
contextualization process, identifying cultural values via the window of proverbs, and  
tailoring discipleship training to incorporate significant aspects of orality.
