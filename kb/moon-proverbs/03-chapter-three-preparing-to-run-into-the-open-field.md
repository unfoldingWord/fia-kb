Introduction  
A rabbit knows that it is important to be well prepared prior to entering the open  
field; otherwise, it will end up with undesirable results \(on the dinner table\). The  
researcher must also have a well-prepared theoretical framework prior to collecting field  
research in order to guide the proper collection and interpretation of the data.  
Theoretical Framework  
The theoretical framework upon which the data are evaluated is founded on four  
major categories\: African primal religious systems \(particularly Hiebert’s framework for  
religious belief systems and Bakhtin’s genre theory\), the constitutive model of  
communication, religious symbols \(particularly Victor Turner’s bipolarization of  
symbols\), and Hiebert’s critical contextualization model. The combination of these  
approaches provides a useful framework to evaluate the potential of proverbs to facilitate  
contextualization in the Builsa church.

Overview  
Hiebert’s three-tiered view of religious belief systems reveals that responses to  
issues arising from primal religion are an urgent need for the church in Africa. In order to  
understand primal religion better, Bakhtin’s genre theory explains that various genres in  
culture highlight certain areas of culture and hide other aspects. Proverbs are an important  
genre that provide valuable insight into understanding Builsa primal religion and culture.  
To understand how proverbs are useful in constructing theology, the constitutive model  
of communication suggests that symbols, such as proverbs, help construct reality in the  
communication process. Proverbs, functioning as religious symbols, can facilitate the  
construction of Builsa Christian theology and ethics. When looking through proverbs like  
windows, aspects of Builsa primal religion and culture are revealed. By adapting  
Hiebert’s critical contextualization model, this understanding gained by Builsa proverbs  
can be engaged with the Buli Scripture, resulting in contextualized expressions of  
Christianity. Builsa proverbs then, provide the Builsa church with a potent tool and  
perspective to facilitate the contextualization of Christianity in Builsa culture.  
African Primal Religion  
Hiebert’s \(1994\:196\) framework of religious belief systems helps me understand  
how large areas of Builsa culture are not addressed by Scripture. While Christianity  
addressed issues at the top level \(High Religion\) and Builsa culture provided answers at  
the lower level \(folk science\), the middle level \(primal religion\) was often excluded or  
ignored when Christianity was presented by missionaries. As a result, issues in the  
“excluded middle level” were addressed with traditional answers given by Builsa primal

Builsa primal religion addresses other areas of life \(middle level\).  
This is analogous to building a house. Missionaries assumed that Christianity

As a result, Christianity and Builsa primal religion operate together to address different  
issues or levels.  
While this “split-level Christianity” has functioned in Builsa culture, it does not  
allow the Scripture to address large areas of life. The contextualization of Christianity in  
Builsa culture is then limited. How can this be corrected? What tools or perspectives can  
be found that will point the way forward for contextualization? What are the roles of  
missionaries and national church leaders/lay people in this process? This study attempts  
to address these questions.  
While Hiebert’s framework of religious belief systems cogently identifies a need

Emerson 1990\:275-276\) explains that each genre provides “a specific way of visualizing  
a given part of reality” since they each combine “specific blindnesses and insights.”

Communication Theory  
While Hiebert’s framework for religious belief systems identified the need for the  
Scripture to engage “middle level issues” in primal religion, and Bakhtin’s genre theory  
points us to look at various genres like proverbs to understand primal religion and  
culture, the constitutive model of communication takes us one step further toward the  
goal of constructing a Builsa Christian worldview. In this model, communication does  
more than pass on a message to a receiver; rather, something may be constructed in the  
process. In the constitutive model, Carey \(1975\:10\) proposed that “communication is a  
symbolic process whereby reality is produced, maintained, repaired, and transformed.”  
When looking at communication in this way, the door is opened to look at how the Builsa  
have constructed the shared inner reality via their symbols. This then prepares the way  
for Builsa to look for symbols that can be useful in reconstructing reality that is both  
Builsa and Christian.  
Our task, then, is to find symbols that are powerful building blocks within Builsa  
culture. Once these are identified, then they can be helpful tools for constructing  
contextual theology. Of course, the Buli language is the most prevalent and pervasive  
symbol system of the Builsa people. Within the Buli language, what symbols exist that  
may contain both the image of God and the problem of sin? What symbols contain deep  
cultural values and serve as windows into the Builsa worldview? The constitutive model  
of communication does not address these questions; however, when the study of religious

Religious Symbols  
When searching for potent symbols to use in constructing theology, religious  
symbols are promising since they provide windows into the soul of a culture. Victor  
Turner’s polarization of symbols identified how symbols bridge both the ideology \(norms  
and values\) and the sensory \(emotions and desires\) poles. Powerful symbols then will be  
those that attach both the deepest ideology of the Builsa culture with the deepest  
emotions.  
Builsa proverbs emerge as religious symbols that do both - they reveal the deep  
norms and values of the Builsa people, and they have an aesthetic function that reaches to  
the deepest senses and becomes “sweet talk.” By combining Turner’s bipolarization of  
symbols with the constitutive model of communication, proverbs are regarded as  
religious symbols that can be potent tools in helping to construct reality for the Builsa  
people. This theoretical framework describes how the proverbs will be understood and  
analyzed in this research - as religious symbols that serve as windows into the soul of the  
Builsa culture. As a result, the proverbs will be studied, as religious symbols, to see what  
they reveal of Builsa culture and primal religion to missionaries and nationals. I will also  
look through this framework to describe how the Builsa respond when proverbs are used  
to communicate Christianity.

The contextualization of Christianity in Builsa culture does not result until the  
Buli Scripture interprets Builsa culture. To take this next step, we come to Hiebert’s  
model of critical contextualization.  
Critical Contextualization  
Hiebert developed the model of critical contextualization, largely to deal with  
cultural problems in the church. His four step process encourages the church to  
understand a cultural issue and then critique it with Scripture. This process includes a  
phenomenological analysis, ontological reflections, critical evaluation, and missiological  
transformations.  
This model can be adapted to deal with proverbs and the cultural issues that  
proverbs reveal. Once Builsa proverbs, functioning as religious symbols, become  
windows to reveal aspects of Builsa culture, this understanding can then be critiqued by  
Scripture \(in the Buli mother tongue\). As a result, the proverbs may become building  
blocks to construct contextual theology.  
This adaptation of Hiebert’s critical contextualization model helps form a  
theoretical framework to address the following questions\: What process can be used in  
Builsa culture to promote contextualization via proverbs? How does this hermeneutical  
community start and operate? Who are the important “construction workers,” and what  
does this process look like? These details have not been adequately described yet in the  
existing literature.

Section Summary  
Hiebert’s analytical framework for religious belief systems helps us see that there  
are many “excluded middle” issues that exist in the Builsa culture that are not addressed  
by Scripture. Bakhtin’s genre theory helps to identify proverbs as a critical, but often  
overlooked, genre that offers unique insight into Builsa culture and primal religion. The  
constitutive model of communication helps us understand how the Builsa construct and  
reconstruct reality via symbols in the language, particularly proverbs. Turner’s  
bipolarization theory of symbols helps identify how proverbs can function as a powerful  
religious symbol set. They are bridges to connect both the deep Builsa ideology and  
sensory poles. They also serve as windows into the soul of the culture. Hiebert’s theory  
of critical contextualization can be adapted for the use of proverbs within a hermeneutical  
community in order to facilitate the construction of theology that is both Builsa and  
Christian.

Measures to Promote Reliability  
In order to strengthen the reliability of the data, multiple data collection methods  
\(as described above\) and multiple data sources were used. The data sources varied in  
their geopraphical locations, formal education levels, and ministry experiences.  
Locations. During the 2004 research, the data were obtained from various  
locations. This included 13 different villages \(8 Builsa, 2 Mamprusi, 1 Konni, and 2  
Kasena/Nankani people groups\). A seminar was also held in the city of Tamale \(a  
Dagomba city in the Northern Region\). In addition, supplementary data was obtained in  
Wilmore, KY with several African students at Asbury Theological Seminary.  
Formal Education Levels. The African participants had various levels of  
education to include\: \(1\) strictly oral learners, \(2\) recently literate in their Mother tongue,  
and \(3\) literate in both their Mother tongue and English. Some had never been to formal  
school while others had completed university training.  
Ministry Experiences. The ministry experiences were also varied to include\: \(1\)  
village lay leaders, \(2\) district chairpersons, and \(3\) national missionaries. The cross-  
cultural workers studied were missionaries in Ghana from the countries of Ghana, United  
States, Australia, England, and South Korea.  
By varying the contexts, education levels, experience, and countries of origin, the  
reliability of the responses to the guiding questions was increased. My long-term  
involvement in the Builsa area also helped to increase the reliability. As the data was  
collected, the analysis was an ongoing process.

Rationale for Narrative Format  
I chose to present the research results in narrative format \(with periodic pauses for  
reflection and analysis\) for the following reasons\:  
1\. The narrative genre is closely related to the genre of proverbs; thus, narrative is  
an appropriate form for this research. In this way, I want to practice what I am preaching.  
I am trying to model that contextual theology in oral cultures like the Builsa may be  
better presented in formats other than propositional, philosophical statements. In addition,  
while I have limited this research to proverbs, it is hoped that other oral literature genres  
will be researched in a similar manner, and I would like to encourage others to consider  
alternate genres for presentation.  
2\. There is precedence for the narrative genre being used in scholarly  
anthropological writing. Dimen \(1977\), as well as Spradley and McDonough \(1973\)  
encouraged the analysis of narrative in order to stimulate anthropological reflection.  
Victor Turner \(1995 \[1969\]\) also presented pages of narrative with periodic commentary  
for reflection and analysis. This added to the authenticity of the writing. Geertz’s \(1973\)  
“thick description” in writings such as “Deep Play\: Notes on the Balinese Cockfight”  
contain narrative that is very engaging as well as insightful. He recreates the context and  
action in which it took place so that the readers participate in the original context, as  
much as possible. This creates an authentic presentation for the reader, while still  
combined with analysis and reflection. These authors have shown that a narrative  
presentation can be combined with reflection and analysis in a way that is scholarly as  
well as engaging.

II Short term missionary, Gerry Lynn, and I wrote this computer database program to  
enter the proverbs with their meanings, appropriate contexts, themes, and appropriate  
Bible references. The data can be entered in English as well as the mother tongue  
language. This program is a database application from the Microsoft Access program.  
This program allows the proverbs to be grouped by themes, words, and biblical passages.  
It also allows the entry of songs and stories in order to combine the analyses with these  
other aspects of oral literature.
