Overview  
To frame the discussion on how Builsa proverbs may be used to engage the  
culture with Scripture, a literature review is required in four major areas\: African Primal  
Religion, communication theory, religious symbols, and critical contextualization. A  
brief overview of each area will be followed by a more detailed review.  
First, it is important to understand religious belief systems in primal cultures in  
order to see how proverbs function in Builsa culture. A review of this literature highlights  
that an understanding of primal religion is an unfinished task of the church in Africa  
today. When looking closer at primal religion, one sees that Builsa primal religion is  
portrayed by various genres that provide different views of reality. One of the genres,  
proverbs, provides an important but often overlooked perspective for understanding  
Builsa primal religion. This frames the problem for us by revealing the need and  
opportunity for proverbs to help engage the Scripture and culture.  
Second, a constitutive model of communication theory is reviewed in order to  
understand how proverbs function in culture to help construct reality. In the constitutive  
model, symbols are a critical link to connect an inner reality \(e.g., the picture in your  
mind of a house\) to an external reality \(e.g., the physical house that you build\), and then  
help communicate this within culture. By viewing how proverbs function as important  
symbols in oral cultures, this highlights the usefulness of proverbs as a readily available

shortfalls, and trends. By combining both the emic and etic perspectives in a  
hermeneutical community via the process of critical contextualization, a greater  
understanding and appreciation of both the Scripture and culture can result. Proverbs then  
become a catalyst to initiate and sustain a process of contextualization. This addresses a  
present and pressing need for contextualization in the Builsa church today.

Religious Belief Systems  
Hiebert \(1994\:196\) provides a framework for the analysis of religious belief  
systems. This framework reveals that issues arising from primal religion are an  
“unfinished agenda” of the church in Africa \(Hiebert 1993\:258\). Heibert \(1994\:197\)  
explains that Western culture has a two-tiered view of reality, due to “the secularization  
of science and the mystification of religion.” On the first level, modern science deals with  
the empirical world to explain that which can be seen and experienced. This addresses  
problems of this world dealing with the natural order or the secular realm. On the second  
level, high religion is left to deal with other-world issues such as faith, miracles, inner  
feelings, and other sacred things.  
In contrast to the West, traditional African culture has a three-tiered view of  
reality. On the first level, folk science deals with the issues of this world that can be  
observed by the senses through the interactions of others. On the third level, high religion  
deals with the forces and beings in the unseen spiritual “other” world that occurred in  
other times. In between these two levels, primal religion addresses forces and beings in  
the unseen world that affect this world. Hiebert \(1994\:196\) calls this level “the excluded  
middle,” since it is “one of the greatest cultural gaps between Western people and many  
traditional religionists.”

the top of the pyramid, the high religion deals with the ultimate issues in the unseen  
spiritual world \(e.g., where we come from or where we go when we die\). In practice, this  
is a small amount of the daily concerns of the average Builsa person. In Builsa culture,

Different Genres  
Language can be divided into different genres or types. Each genre has its own  
unique perspective that describes reality for people within a culture. Bakhtin1 explains,  
“Certain features of language take on the specific flavor of a given genre\: they knit  
together with specific points of view, specific approaches, forms of thinking, nuances and  
accents characteristic of the given genre” \(Holquist 1981\:289\). As a result, Bakhtin  
continues, each genre provides “a specific way of visualizing a given part of reality”  
since they each combine “specific blindnesses and insights” \(Morson and Emerson  
1990\:275-276\). While language unifies a people and their perspective of reality, genres  
also serve to stratify language and culture such that each different genre provide a  
different perspective \(Holquist 1981\:429\). When taken together, a more complete picture  
of the cultural view of reality is given.  
The different genre together form a set of blueprints \(Hiebert 1999\:76-81\) or  
mental maps \(Spradley 1979\:7\) that provide a “guide for acting and for interpreting our  
experience.” Each drawing in the set of blueprints “purports to give us true information,  
but only about some part of reality” \(Hiebert 1999\:80\). Korzybski \(1933\) introduced the  
metaphor that the verbal world is related to the extensional world \(that which is not  
directly experienced by the senses, but only heard about or learned\) as a map does to the  
territory it is supposed to represent. Verbal words, then, are symbols or mental maps that

people carry in their heads. Various genres provide the material to form various maps of  
the same territory \(e.g., one map shows roads while another shows population density and  
another shows political boundaries, etc.\). These genres combine to provide a more

cultures exhibit a rich repertoire of genres, offering a wide range of particularized visions

Genre of Proverbs  
Proverbs are a critical genre to help understand Builsa culture and primal religion.  
Van Rheenen \(1991\:39\) discusses how “oral cultures, which are prevalent in Third World  
societies, are proverb-oriented.. proverbs and myths are significantly helpful in  
deciphering animistic worldviews.” He then adds, “To learn as much of the new culture  
as possible, a new cross-cultural worker must learn the oral forms of his adopted people,  
in particular proverbs and myths” \(1991\:44\). Seidensticker \(1987\:8\) adds, “Proverbs are a  
compact treatise on the values of culture,” but again no details are given concerning what  
process can be followed. John Pobee \(Nussbaum 1996-98\) explained that “proverbs are a  
vital and important mode of communicating and key to penetrating the worldview of  
Africans.” Pobee \(1979\:75-77\) goes a step further than many others by illustrating the  
natural revelation of God in Akan culture with various proverbs. Kudadjie \(1996\:14\)  
affirms that “The collective thought, beliefs, and values of an African people can be  
discerned from their proverbs,” and he gives examples of the use of proverbs in  
preaching. These are good steps but they do not go far enough to explain to the Builsa  
church how they can use proverbs to begin developing contextual theology.

nineteenth century” \(Hildebrandt 1996\:103\). In a letter to Henry Venn in 1862  
concerning the tragic loss of his papers in a house fire, Crowther \(Hastings 1994\:279\)

Communication Theory  
Communication theory can help explain how proverbs function in Builsa culture  
as symbols. Symbols help to construct reality and communicate this within a culture.  
They then serve as a link to connect the inner view of reality with the external reality.  
Communication Defined/Delimited  
It is difficult to find a single unified definition of communication. Dance and  
Larson \(1972\) reviewed the literature over 30 years ago and identified 126 different  
definitions. Since that time, several definitions have been added and the field of  
communications has significantly broadened. Noting the close relationship between

understanding culture and the process of communication, Hall \(1959\:217\) notes, “Culture  
is communication and communication is culture.” Littlejohn \(2002\:3\) notes, “Much  
disagreement exists about what constitutes an adequate theory of communication” since  
“communication is one of the most pervasive, important, and complex aspects of human  
life.” He advocates looking at all of the theories and then selecting those which are most  
helpful for a particular study.  
Transmission model. Communication has been commonly defined as the  
“transmission or exchange of ideas” \(Craig 2001\:125\). This transmission model “is  
conceptualized as a process in which meaning, packaged in symbolic messages like  
bananas in crates, are transported from sender to receiver” \(Craig 2001\:125\) with  
interference in between. While this description may be criticized as a caricature, it serves  
to highlight the main elements of the transmission model\: sender \(source\), message, and  
receiver. Aristotle \(Kennedy 1991\) is the early classical advocate of the Source-Message-  
Receiver transmission model, where he dealt with the ethos of the speaker, the logos of  
the message, and the pathos of the receivers.  
Recently, the transmission model has been critiqued by others who claim that  
communication is more complex than previously thought. For example, Relevance theory  
shows that context is as important as text. Instead of transmitting information between  
people, Relevance Theory sees communication “as a matter of enlarging mutual cognitive  
environments, not of duplicating thoughts” \(Sperber and Wilson 1995\:193\). In this view,  
people selectively pay attention to stimuli based upon what they consider to be relevant.  
Hill \(2003\) found Relevance Theory helped explain how cross-cultural communication

was often laden with contextual mismatches, which required contextual adjustment  
materials. This insight is not apparent using the transmission model.  
This transmission model is helpful to explain why some proverbs are more readily  
appreciated than other proverbs in particular audiences with particular speakers. I have  
heard Builsa say that people will not take you seriously if you do not know and use  
proverbs in speech. The proverbs carry forward the authority of the ancestors, which  
gives weight to the ethos of the messenger. The use of proverbs also ties into the deeper  
aspects of cultural understanding such that a connection is made with the pathos of the  
Builsa audience. One Builsa said, “When you speak in proverbs, it is like you are singing  
a song that I want to hear. You can sing that song all day long and I will listen.” While  
these aspects of proverbs are helpful, the focus of this research is contextualization of  
Christianity. For this focus, another communication model may be more helpful.  
Constitutive model. Some communication theorists are now favoring a  
“constitutive model” as an alternative to the classical transmission model. Craig  
\(2001\:125\) notes,  
The constitutive model posits instead that the elements of communication, rather  
than being fixed in advance, are reflexively constituted within the act of  
communication itself. Communication is defined as an ongoing process that  
symbolically forms and re-forms our personal identities, our social relations, our  
common world of meaningful objects and events, our ideas and feelings, and our  
routine ways of expressing these socially constructed realities.. .The field of  
communication expands to include all aspects of the creation and negotiation of  
meaning in society.  
The constitutive model of communication recognizes that communication often does

process of communication. Symbols are a critical building block to connect the inner  
reality with the external reality for people. In dialogue together, person A and person B  
use symbols to communicate. The traffic is two-way since symbols are both used and  
received. In the process, the symbols are affirmed, modified, or rejected in this dialogue.

reality, even though the inner reality of person A \(thought 1\) and the inner reality of  
person B \(thought 2\) do not *exactly* correspond to each other, or to the external reality for  
that matter. The point is that the symbols are continually negotiated through dialogue  
within community since they are never exact representations. This concept will be useful

Oral Cultures  
Proverbs, functioning as symbols, are especially important in oral cultures where  
discussions must be remembered in order to be useful. Ong \(1982\) emphasizes that oral  
cultures rely upon formulaic expressions and mnemonic patterns in order for people to  
remember what was said. Proverbs are particularly useful in oral cultures since they are  
often short, pithy statements that are easy to remember. McLuhan \(1964\) emphasizes that  
the primary channel or medium of communication changes the way people perceive  
themselves. His catch phrase was “The medium is the message,” and he emphasized the  
changes in thinking from oral to written to electronic media \(especially television\). These  
distinctives have often been overlooked by missionaries from highly literate cultures.  
Kennedy \(2001\) discusses how the cross cultural study of rhetoric allows  
comparisons between cultures that helps identify features in one culture that may not  
exist in another. In societies without writing, he notes \(2001\:138\), “there is much  
repetition in most traditional speeches and little explicit logical reasoning, though  
references to myth, legend, history, and proverbs may supply examples of what should be  
done in support of the speaker’s proposition.” He \(2001\:142\) adds,  
A complex chain of logical argument is not a feature of persuasion in nonliterate  
societies. Argument from examples - mythological, historical, experiential, or  
analogical - has probably been the most common form of reasoning all over the  
world, and proverbs are a basis of demonstration in many cultures, though often  
universal propositions are not articulated.  
This indicates one reason that those from a literate background may overlook the value  
that oral cultures place upon proverbs. The logic and repetition of proverbs is highly  
valued in oral cultures but it is not generally appreciated in literate cultures.

Symbols Bridge  
Victor Turner found that symbols polarized meaning between two poles\: sensory  
and ideology. Turner \(1967a\:28\) explained these terms, “At the sensory pole are  
concentrated those significata that may be expected to arouse desires and feelings; at the  
ideological pole one finds an arrangement of norms and values that guide and control  
persons as members of social groups.” Hiebert et al \(1999\:247\) discuss sacred symbols  
using somewhat different terms, but conveying a similar meaning, by noting that  
they integrate and give expression to a people’s worldview - the mental picture  
they have of the way things in reality actually are \(the cognitive dimension\); the  
tone, character, and aesthetic quality of their life \(the affective dimension\); and  
the moral standards that set their ideals and governs their relationships \(the  
evaluative dimensions\).

has largely been overlooked by most researchers; instead, they have focused on studying  
either the sensory or the ideology poles but not both together.  
Several studies focus largely on the didactic and normative value of proverbs  
\(Christensen 1958; Shelton 1963\), and a few on the aesthetic value of proverbs \(Boadi

in a certain historical age that may change; also, they can corrupt those with power by  
maintaining their privilege. This has implications for both culture change and power  
abuse/prejudice in the Builsa culture \(to be discussed in the next section\). Many proverbs  
fit the characteristics of deep symbols, which again helps to highlight their importance in  
Builsa culture.

Section Summary  
Religious symbols open levels of reality to experience Builsa religion and culture  
that would otherwise be hidden. When proverbs are looked *through* instead of merely  
looked *at,* they can function as religious symbols. This then opens windows into  
understanding and experiencing Builsa religion and culture that would otherwise be  
closed. Since proverbs are so prevalent in Builsa culture, they become windows that are  
open every day. Once opened, these windows invite fresh areas of discovery.  
Since symbols combine both the sensory and ideological aspects, they affect both  
the head and the heart. Proverbs, functioning as symbols, can facilitate contextualization  
that reaches both the head and heart of the Builsa. As deep symbols, proverbs reach deep  
within culture and carry the potential to touch the deeper aspects of the Builsa worldview.  
That is the level that is necessary to reach in order to overcome “split-level Christianity.”  
Proverbs have the potential to address significant aspects of “split-level  
Christianity.” While many concur with the powerful potential of proverbs to do this, none  
give details as to how this can be done. This research fills this gap. Before  
contextualization can occur, however, the Scripture must engage these deeper aspects of  
Builsa culture. That process is the topic of our next discussion.  
Contextualization in Africa  
Contextualization occurs as the mother tongue Scripture and culture are engaged,  
leading to a deeper understanding and appreciation of both. This facilitates a contextual  
understanding and communication of Christian truth. While contextualization is highly  
discussed, a survey of previous efforts reveals that there are few results to show for this  
**R eproduced with perm ission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without perm ission.** 64  
effort. New tools and perspectives are needed - proverbs can be a catalyst to initiate the  
way forward. The neglect of symbols, such as proverbs, has closed windows that need to  
be opened.  
Neglecting Symbols  
Since symbols are valuable windows into the soul of a culture, the neglect of them  
overlooks the indigenous inner view of reality. In theology, this hampers the  
understanding of Christian truth, as previously noted by several African theologians.  
Yankah \(1982\) found that Africans trained in the West tended not to use proverbs  
frequently, which then put them at a disadvantage when returning home where proverbial  
wisdom is held in high esteem. To understand how the neglect of African symbols such  
as proverbs affect the understanding of Christian truth, a brief history of the recent  
development of African contextual theology is helpful.  
Recent History  
Ritchie \(1993\) traced the development of African contextual theology in four  
phases\: rejection, adaptation, incarnation, and emerging theologies. In the first phase  
\(Rejection\), with few exceptions, African traditional beliefs, practices, and religion were  
considered evil and of no value to Christianity. For example, in the 1910 Edinburgh  
World Missionary Conference \(World 1910\), it was felt that African traditional religion  
contained no preparation for Christianity. As a result, the attitude was that the  
missionaries must start over with a clean slate *\(tabula rasa*\) and start to teach universal  
Christianity. It was not realized at the time that the “universal Christianity” was actually a  
Western contextualized expression of Christianity.

Roland Allen started to write about the self-supporting church \(1962\[1912\]\), but  
his thoughts largely concentrated on the relationship between the church to the mission  
and did not consider the development of contextual theology. In 1926, Edwin Smith  
wrote The Golden Stool, which suggested that “Christianity should be presented as not  
antagonistic to, but the fulfillment of, African culture, religion, and tradition.” \(Hastings  
1994\:553-554\). In 1934, the first Catholic clergy to be entirely staffed by Africans with  
no missionary control was in Buganda, but “its clergy were meticulously faithful to the  
“Statutes” regulating the details of their daily life, drawn up on missionary authority”  
\(Hastings 1994\:571-575\). This was necessary in order to survive the experiment amidst  
colonialism and missionary hesitancy. Hence, contextual theology was not a topic under  
discussion.  
Contextualization received a boost with the publication of Placide Tempels’ book,  
Bantu Philosophy \(Tempels 1959\). This was originally published in French in 1945, and  
later translated into English. Parratt \(1995\:10\) notes,  
It was the first attempt to come to grips, other than in a purely descriptive way,  
with African thought forms, and to treat them both analytically and as a coherent  
system. It also set out to show the importance of the study of language and oral  
lore, as well as the accepted methods of ethnology, in bringing to light religious  
and philosophical ideas. In particular, Tempels’ identification of “life-force” as  
the ground of Bantu ontology has contributed to our understanding of African  
thought in general and has provided a base from which several of his successors  
worked.  
Tempels’ work was important in that it started to take African philosophy seriously. His  
work was followed by Catholic African priests, Kagame, Lulufiiabo, and Mulago. Parratt  
\(1995\:10\) notes that Kagame’s work, La Philosphie Bantu Rwandaise de l’Etre “made  
wide-ranging use of linguistics and oral tradition.” While these efforts began to

understand African philosophy as a coherent system of thought, there was no discussion  
of developing a contextual theology.  
In the second phase \(Adaptation\), for the most part, certain outward forms began  
to be adapted to the local culture. In 1963, the All Africa Conference of Churches was  
founded in Kampala. The focus here was on modifying local forms \(e.g., music, dress,  
and involving African clergy\) but this was still done within the framework of Western  
theology. It merely became Western theology with African clothes. Vatican II started to  
look again at theology and culture. Isichei \(1995\:327\) noted, “Vatican II encouraged a  
contextual theology, and a more positive approach to other faith traditions.” The 1968  
Kinshasa Colloquium is a notable example. “Its conclusions were epitomized in  
Mulago’s affirmation that there could be no universal theology, and that it was no longer  
possible to put the clock back and deny to African theologians the freedom to elaborate a  
Christian theology that would not be foreign to the African milieu, tradition of thought,  
mentality, and culture” \(Parratt 1995\:11\). While this affirmation was not fully realized, it  
was prophetic concerning the upcoming trend in the development of contextual  
theologies.  
Ritchie \(1993\) describes the third phase of African contextual theology in the  
1970s as Incarnation. At that time, a host of conferences were convened to include the  
All Africa Council of Churches \(AACC\), the WCC, the Lutheran World Federation, the  
Ecumenical Association of African Theologians and the Ecumenical Association for  
Third World Theologians \(EATWOT\). In 1974, the AACC called for a moratorium of  
Western theologians in Africa. This was significant in that it showed how far Africa had  
come. They had trained theologians who were not content with Western expressions of  
**R eproduced with perm ission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without perm ission.** 67  
Christianity; thus, they wanted space to theologize without Western domination. Africans  
began to research African traditional religion and become aware of their religio-cultural  
roots \(Ritchie 1993\). In addition, the EATWOT conference in 1976 was convened since  
the Third World churches did not want to be defined by the rich, the Westerners, and the  
Whites. This was a clear backlash against the Western global theology, and it set the  
stage for the further development of contextual theologies.  
In the fourth phase \(Emerging Theologies\), much is still unknown. There is not a  
single paradigm and it is still emerging \(Ritchie 1993\) since “African theology is in the  
midst of its birth pangs” \(Penoukou 1991\:28\). The word “theologies” is plural since this  
recognizes the different cultural contexts that exist in Africa. The current trends seem to  
be that Africans are striving to address cultural issues as a community of believers in the  
mother tongue. They are using African symbols, themes, oral literature, etc. and their  
own mother tongue categories for theologizing.  
John Pobee is a good example. His approach “will involve the collection,  
analysis, and elucidation of oral literature - myths, prayers, proverbs, and so on. The raw  
materials of African theology thus are biblical-theological on the one hand, and the oral  
traditions of African religion on the other” \(Parratt 1995\:49\). Proverbs then are valuable  
indigenous resources for African theologians to work with in developing contextual  
theologies. Parratt \(1995\:50\) comments on Pobee,  
One outstanding feature of his book is his adroit use of Akan proverbs and  
sayings, by which he attempts to bring out the folk wisdom of Akan  
religion...few have so painstakingly delved in religion and moral oral traditions  
as Pobee does here. In this he has opened up new possibilities for exploring  
common ground between African religions and the Christian faith.

Wav Forward in Contextual Theology  
In searching for a contemporary paradigm for mission, Bosch \(1991\:431\) suggests  
that the best models of contextual theology should hold together theory and praxis, while  
also incorporating *poiesis.* Stackhouse \(1988\:85\) defines *poiesis* as “an imaginative  
creation or representation of evocative images.” Bosch notes that this includes the  
elements of “beauty, the rich resources of symbol, piety, worship, love, awe, and  
mystery.” Bosch \(1991\:431\) adds, “Only too often , in the tug-of-war between the  
priority of truth and the priority of justice, this dimension gets lost.” Bosch advocates the  
use of symbols in theology \(since this has largely been ignored or misunderstood in the  
past\), which cracks open the door to explore proverbs and other oral literature;  
unfortunately, he does not provide any concrete process that can facilitate contextual  
theology.  
Others are also starting to grasp the need to understand and apply symbols in  
contextual theology. Hiebert, Shaw, and Tienou \(1999\:254\) comment, “Contextualization

of the gospel in other cultures must begin at the level of signs.” Schreiter \(1985\:28\) also  
affirmed,  
Ideally, for a genuinely contextual theology, the theological process should begin  
with the opening of culture, that long and careful listening to a culture to discover  
its principal values, needs, interests, directions, and symbols. Only in this way  
can the configurations of a culture become apparent of themselves, without  
simply responding to other kinds of needs extrinsic to the culture.  
Schreiter \(1985\:29\) advocates analyzing the cultural symbols in order to find the activity  
of Christ in the culture long before the arrival of the missionary. This approach seeks to  
develop a local theology as the local hermeneutical community “listen\[s\] to its culture in  
order to complement its past experience of Christ. It must be able to recognize the signs

proverbs in culture, in order to find the presence of Christ. Still, the yearning to do so  
exists in Africa.  
The plea for discovering and using symbols in African contextual theology is  
poignantly summarized by Afagbegee’s poem, “Cry of an African Christian” \(2001\:66\),  
where he issues “a plea for understanding and sensitivity towards his symbols,” and he  
utters “a groan for patient-love for his mode of expressing himself.” The emotion of this  
poem illustrates the huge gap between the desire for the use of local symbols in  
theologizing and its neglect in practice. A process must be discovered that will fill this  
gap. The use of African communication forms points us in the right direction.

To answer this question, Nussbaum \(2000\) analyzes the wisdom paradigms of

used more in theologizing if the African wisdom paradigm is appreciated and  
encouraged.  
Clearly, appropriate cultural symbols are essential in developing contextual  
theology. Builsa proverbs, functioning as symbols, are important resources for  
theologizing. In addition to the content, proverbs are a communication form that is highly  
esteemed and appreciated in Builsa culture. As a result, they can be an appropriate form  
to help communicate Builsa theology. In order for this to occur, however, there must be  
an engagement of both the cultural proverbs and the Scripture.

As instruments of corrupted power, deep symbols can mirror the society’s  
stratification of privilege. All actual societies privilege some members over

symbols.

power over women. This is an important critique and shows how the proverbs must be  
critically evaluated instead of passively accepted.

Section Summary  
A review of the recent history of African contextual theology reveals the  
shortfalls, trends, and recent progress. A clear way forward to promote contextualization  
is still lacking. While symbols were largely ignored in the past since they were not exact  
or scientific, the analysis of cultural symbols is a recommended starting place by several  
adherents of contextual theology. Proverbs, functioning as a symbol system, can be a  
useful starting point for contextualization. In addition to content, proverbs are an  
appropriate form to communicate theology in many African cultures.  
While the process of critical contextualization was not originally designed for the  
analysis of proverbs, it can be adapted to do so. As the cultural perspective gained by  
proverbs is critically engaged with Scripture, new avenues for contextualization may be  
realized. While some are starting to do this, these efforts are still very young and lacking  
in detail. This research attempts to fill in many of the details. By combining both the emic
