Overview  
Through the preceding narrative, I have demonstrated how Builsa proverbs can  
facilitate the contextualization of Christianity in Builsa culture. Now that the narrative is  
completed, it is time to reflect on the major missiological lessons that can be learned. I  
will discuss five main areas. First, the important \(but often overlooked\) role of oral  
literature in primary oral cultures will he highlighted. Secondly, it will be shown how  
proverbs provide a good starting place to encourage contextualization. Third, a summary  
of how proverbs can promote an engagement of Scripture and Builsa culture will be  
discussed. Fourth, I will discuss some important factors to consider for a process to  
facilitate contextualization. Finally, implications will be given for the training of  
missionaries and church leaders.  
Oralitv  
Many missionaries who enter a culture with high illiteracy will presume that there  
is not a large quantity of literature in the culture. This is often quite wrong! There is much  
literature in the Builsa culture, but it is transmitted orally and not via the written page.  
This omission is quite understandable since missionaries are usually formally educated  
and trained in literate cultures; therefore, they overlook many unique aspects of primary  
oral cultures.

aspects of oral cultures when he observed, “Perhaps in the matter of esthetic cultures

Ong \(1982\) has described how primary oral cultures, like the Builsa, have very  
elaborate and ornate ways of speaking that are often misunderstood and overlooked by  
those in literate cultures \(see the chart in Appendix F\), thereby creating misunderstanding  
and miscommunication. The narrative was meant to show some of this oral art in the  
context of Builsa conversation so that readers will begin to recognize and appreciate its  
value. This was kept in the context of Builsa conversation since, “Modem folklorists who  
study proverbs assert that a proverb’s meaning cannot be wholly understood apart from  
the context of its usage” \(Eastman 1972\:193-194\). Clearly, there is more occurring in the  
use of a proverb than just the meaning of the words would suggest. A few characteristics  
of oral literature will be summarized from the narrative.  
Literature in a literate culture is locked into a given historical period at the time of  
writing. Oral literature, on the other hand, is constantly adapted to fit the new context and  
the speaker is expected to use creativity to adapt the message to the specific context

\(Finnegan 1970\:9\). This aspect of proverbs makes them fruitful material for  
contextualization. Notice how a proverb like “Where the heart is, there the feet will

Jesus’ protection of and desire for his people in a way that is attractive and  
understandable to the Builsa. This adaptability of proverbs to fit new contexts is an  
important and useful feature of proverbs.  
Another feature of the Buli language that can be used to produce a beautiful  
performance is its tonal nature. This can help produce a performance similar to a rhyme  
or song in a Western language. Buli has three different tones - low, middle and high. The  
same word spoken in the three different tones has three completely different meanings.  
When the tones are carefully considered and placed in proverbs, it can greatly enhance  
the aesthetic appeal of the proverb. Notice the rising and falling tones indicated by the  
horizontal lines in the proverb, *“Popo j iib alaajii ngmamk jiib*.” This creates a pleasing  
affect that sounds like a song as the words *“jii”* or *“jiib”* have elevated tones. This quality  
is not available in English.  
Another quality that is not a part of the English language is the use of noun  
classes. The proper use of noun classes can be used to enhance the aesthetic expression.  
Buli has five noun classes, and every noun is placed in one of these categories or classes.  
The pronoun “it” can be said in five different ways, depending on which noun class it

that are normally given to humans should be withheld so that the child can face the  
consequences of their actions. This is subtle \(note how close the two words “Aa” and  
“wa” sound\) but this nuance enhances the verbal performance.

Proverbs can also conjure up concrete, vivid images. Boadi \(1972\:185\) explains,  
“The more concrete and unusual the image, the higher the proverb rates.” Notice the  
vivid imagery in the proverb, *“Fi dan sak bulorik dueni deka, ku chum le faari fi ma*” \(If  
you teach an ugly man how to marry women, in the future he will marry your mother\). It  
makes the listener stop and think, usually accompanied by laughter. The same occurs  
with the proverb, *“Fi dan pa poning a poni nuru zuk, wa chum le pa takaribaling a poni*  
*f i zu k*’ \(If you take a pair of scissors and cut someone’s hair, in the future he will take a  
piece of broken pot and cut your hair\). The vivid, striking images enhance the appeal and  
effectiveness of the proverbs.  
These striking images address issues in an oblique or indirect manner instead of  
directly. This *biik-wiani* or *biik-sobilik* communication is highly valued amoung the  
Builsa and it helps the Builsa to know that this conversation has weight that adults should  
consider. When Christianity is expressed in this manner, the message then becomes oral  
art that can attract a mature audience. When missionaries learn language, they often learn  
to speak in a direct manner \(i.e., *biik-pioluk* \). Without knowing it, the missionaries use of  
*biik-pioluk* has hindered the spread of Christianity. Adeney \(1985\:397\) argues that early  
missionary training should give significant attention to indigenous literary genres since,  
“over the long run, missionaries will communicate more effectively if they use available,  
understandable, and valued media.”  
Still another feature of Builsa oral literature is the use of ideophones \(words that  
sound like the object\). Finnegan \(1970\:66\) observes how the ideophone is used in oral

literature as “a rhetorical and emotive tool whose effectiveness cannot be  
overemphasized.” Consider proverbs such as, *“Kuri, kuri kan wari vorub po*” \(\[The  
sound of\] Kuri, kuri will not stay inside a hole\). While the Buli word *“kurf* ’ has no  
meaning in the Buli language by itself, it is used to sound like something that is rustling  
around in a hole in the ground \(such as a snake, mouse, etc.\). This forces the hearer of the  
proverb to think back to when they have heard that sound. An ideophone is also used in  
the proverb, “*Kilima, kilima kan jogi nyain”* \(Hurry, hurry brings nyain \[the sound of a  
breaking pot\]\). Again, this is a descriptive way to force the listener to remember when  
they have heard the distinct sound of “nyain” when a pot broke.  
The above discussion on oral literature is not meant to be exhaustive; rather, it is  
meant to show some of the distinct characteristics of oral literature when compared to  
written literature. Many of these characteristics are overlooked by those who come from  
literate cultures. They are right under their feet and they do not notice them since they are  
not used to looking for them. An understanding and appreciation for oral literature as an  
oral art form, however, prepares a literate person to look for and appropriate proverbs and  
other oral literature for effective communication and contextualization in the Builsa  
culture.  
Some are starting to take note of the fact that the majority of people in the world  
are oral learners, while mission is often conducted in a literate manner. Sogaard  
\(2004\:11-12\) recently observed,  
Still, almost everything we do in church and mission is based on a linear print  
orientation. Even follow up material to radio programs is in print, study material  
is in print, and the way we teach new pastors to prepare sermons is based on a  
linear model which does not make sense to the non-reading population. The sad  
thing is that most pastors do not seem to take this situation seriously.

The narrative describes what can happen as church leaders take orality seriously  
and use proverbs to express and contextualize Christianity in the Builsa culture. It is  
hoped that this research would encourage other groups to do the same. The details  
provided in the narrative are meant to offer some practical suggestions for future  
discovery. In response to the challenge that orality presents to the church, Sogaard  
\(2004\:12\) suggests, “First, we have to motivate church and mission leaders by making  
them aware of the situation and its implications for the future. We also need to prepare  
material that is based on oral communication principles.” I hope this research motivates  
church and mission leaders, and it would create an awareness of the possibilities that  
exist when oral literature \(such as proverbs\) is used in the church.  
One final word needs to be said about orality and possible materials to use. Oral  
literature includes various genres that each filter out and highlight various aspects of  
culture. As a result, various genres should be explored, instead of focusing on one to the  
exclusion of the others. This is to say, that there is no “silver bullet” when it comes to  
oral literature and contextualization. Proverbs, by themselves, serve valuable functions  
but they are not complete without other genres. In the same way, other genres, such as  
story, are not sufficient by themselves either.  
I bring up this point about orality since some ministries have latched onto  
storytelling as a response to orality with the hope that this is the “cure-all.” One of the  
Lausanne 2004 Forum Summary Affirmations \(Committee 2004\:1\) states,  
We acknowledge the reality that much of the world is made up of oral learners  
who understand best when information comes to them by means of stories. A  
large proportion of the world’s populations are either unable to or unwilling to  
absorb information through written communications. Therefore, a need exists to

Acknowledging the reality of orality is a necessary first step forward, but it does not go  
far enough. While stories are useful and appropriate for oral cultures, other genres like  
proverbs provide tremendous potential for discipleship, evangelism, counseling, and  
contextualization, as witnessed in the narrative. Stories may be an attractive solution to  
the issues of orality since Westerners often recognize the value of storytelling. Proverbs

Westerners but are attractive to and appreciated by oral learners such as the Builsa. The  
discussion in this section above is meant to point out some aspects of oral literature that  
English speakers may not readily notice, but they can be effectively used in  
contextualization. Instead of being overlooked, proverbs should be used in conjunction  
with other genres such as stories. After all - proverbs are right under our feet.  
Finding a Starting Place  
The narrative demonstrates that contextualization occurs when Scripture and  
culture are engaged by using the mother tongue Scripture as the interpreter of culture in a  
hermeneutical community. While this is easy to say, it is very difficult to practice,  
especially in the midst of a crisis like the death of Immanuel’s son. If the church leaders  
are not familiar with the process of contextualization, then the church often falls back on  
the two common responses of acceptance or rejection.  
In order to get beyond these two common responses, the narrative also  
demonstrates how proverbs can be a good starting place to learn the process of

contextualization. The Builsa proverbs are familiar ground that the Builsa enjoy  
discussing, they promote discovery, and they are laden with cultural insights. For the  
Builsa, these proverbs act like a mirror to reflect back to them aspects of their culture that  
they may not normally discuss, such as witchcraft, attitudes toward marriage

As these attitudes are brought to the surface, then the church can dig into the root  
issues and values that produce these in the Builsa culture. Just as a tree \(what you see  
above ground\) only exists because of the roots \(what you do not see below ground\), the  
proverbs and resulting attitudes only exist because of the underlying cultural values. By  
discussing proverbs, many of these roots can be exposed. It is at this point that serious  
contextualization can occur, as the Buli Scripture interprets these roots. Proverbs are a  
catalyst to birth contextualization. They are a good place to start for contextualization  
since they are attractive, familiar, and fully Builsa. They fit the Builsa context well.  
Beyond the Builsa area, a good place to start for contextualization is also needed  
in the wider church. For example, while it is easy to talk about contextualization in the  
Western church, it is very difficult to practice and the results are not encouraging.  
Westerners as well as Builsa need to find a process whereby they can engage

Scripture with their culture. While it sounds easy to do, it may take practice in some  
familiar territory before Americans will be ready to handle the more difficult  
contextualization challenges.  
Presently, the church in America is uncertain how to respond to issues like  
homosexuality, abortion, and even Harry Potter. A good starting place for  
contextualization needs to be found and utilized so that the church will learn how to “ride  
the bike.” Once they get used to the process of riding the bike, then these more difficult  
hills can be addressed in a way that is fully Scriptural and still appropriate to the  
American cultural context.  
While proverbs may be a helpful starting place for contextualization in the  
American context, other genres should be explored that fit the contemporary American  
culture. A church in Lexington, KY has used popular movies as a good starting place for  
contextualization. After watching the movies together, the participants then discuss some  
of the root issues and use Scripture to interpret them. It is hoped that, as the church gets  
used to the process of contextualization by the familiar genre of movies, then they will be  
better prepared with a process to use for tackling more difficult contextualization  
challenges up ahead. The next section will describe how this process using movies may  
catalyze contextualization in the U.S. similar to the way proverbs were a catalyst in  
Buluk.  
Engagement of Scripture and Culture  
While analyzing proverbs provides a process to use for contextualization, the  
bigger fish to catch is the engagement of Scripture with cultural issues. The narrative

In the United States, churches have been deeply divided over the Harry Potter  
books and movies. This has been a very controversial issue, as Matlock noted, “Nothing  
can divide a roomful of Christians \[in the West\] like the Harry Potter debate” \(Matlock  
2003\:24\). Well meaning and respected Christians are sharply divided in their opinions.  
Neal \(2001\) summarizes the dilemma by the title of her book, “What’s a Christian to do  
with Harry Potter?” The typical responses are very similar to those that I found in the  
Builsa culture with issues related to the drum ban, etc. Often, people responded by total  
rejection or total acceptance, without allowing a process to occur that would engage  
Scripture with culture.  
After returning to the U.S. after being away four years, a Christian friend warned  
me, “Don’t let your kids watch the Harry Potter movies. They are full of witchcraft and  
evil!” Our feet had hardly touched the ground when this stem warning was given to us.  
Being parents of four kids, we were naturally concerned and wanted to protect them from  
sources of evil. We were returning from living in the Builsa area, and we were sensitive  
to the serious influences of evil in daily life. Naturally, this warning about Harry Potter  
received our serious attention.  
When I asked another friend \(who was a graduate of a reputable seminary\), he  
advised in the opposite direction, “There is nothing wrong with Harry Potter. It is just a  
good story. This is part of our culture and I can’t shelter my kids from it. They need to

church can use to engage Scripture with culture, resulting in contextualization.  
The process of involving an interpretive community that we saw in the Builsa  
church may be just as helpful in a Western setting when seeking to respond to the Harry  
Potter movies. Westerners often place a high value on individual freedom and choices.

Holy Spirit desires to speak through the broader Christian community. It is inadequate to  
place the decision about watching Harry Potter movies in the hands of individual families  
alone. Yet, *Campus Life* \(a Christian magazine written for Western teenagers\) recently

Christ United Methodist Church in Lexington, KY, exemplifies one approach that  
a church can take. A Wednesday night group met at the church to discuss “The Gospel  
According to Pop Culture.” In this format, a small interpretive community was formed  
that watched 30 minutes of a movie and then spent the next 30 minutes in discussion.  
Scripture was used to interpret the content of the movie. Recently, when viewing the  
movie “Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone,” questions were asked such as\: What  
characteristics does Harry demonstrate that are compatible with characteristics  
considered virtuous and acceptable in the Christian faith? Who are the Voldermorts of  
our day and age, and what qualities do they have? What kind of trolls do we face in our  
lives? How is Harry’s solution to the troll problem similar to our solutions? How would  
you use scenes and events from this movie segment to help explain Christian beliefs to a  
person who is not familiar with the church, the Bible, or Christian theology? They are  
using these questions to dig down to some of the roots and identify values in American  
culture.

Another helpful step forward may be in the articulation of epistemological  
differences. Perhaps, the Harry Potter battles are more about differences in epistemology

toward resolution. The extremes of rejection or acceptance are often justified and  
strengthened by both parties. Instead of the Christian community moving towards

While the key role of Scripture has been discussed, it should be noted that the use  
of the mother tongue \(vernacular\) Scripture is important. When using the Buli translation  
of the Bible to address the drum ban, the *teng nyono* was surprised and encouraged to  
hear that the Bible was relevant to his issues. The vernacular translation allowed God to  
speak in terms and concepts that the Builsa could understand. In the same way, the  
contemporary vernacular translations of the Bible may be helpful in the Harry Potter  
discussions by using terms and concepts that are familiar to teens of today.  
Whether in Buluk amidst a drumming ban or in the U.S. during the “Harry Potter  
wars,” issues of Scripture and culture will always be with the church. There are no easy  
or quick answers to many of these issues. Fortunately, there are tools to guide us in the  
process of engaging Scripture with culture so that we remain faithful to Scripture as well  
as relevant to culture. The Builsa narrative provides a process that can be helpful in the  
U.S. also.  
Historian Adrian Hastings noted that discussions concerning issues like  
polygamy, bride wealth, circumcision, etc., in the African church, in the long run, have  
been an instrument to focus on what being African and Christian means. He concludes,  
“Christianity has often thrived upon internal argument” \(1994\:322\). In other words,  
authentic expressions of African Christianity thrive upon the arguments \(dialogue\)  
created when Scripture and culture are critically engaged. Likewise, authentic  
expressions of American Christianity may thrive upon the dialogue created by the  
engagement of Scripture with cultural issues such as those in the Harry Potter movies.

The problem that often occurs is that well-meaning Christians often focus on the

Contextualization Process  
We have been searching up and down the river for a process that can facilitate  
contextualization. Through the narrative, we have identified a process that was helpful to  
the Builsa churches. In order to broaden the applications beyond Buluk, the following  
key factors have been identified that facilitate the contextualization process. These are  
summarized below\:  
1\. A catalyst is often helpful to initiate and sustain the process of  
contextualization. This includes a person or persons who can organize people, and  
facilitate discussion/consensus. They also pay attention to logistic details.  
2\. A hermeneutical community provides a wider and more informed perspective  
for the Holy Spirit to speak. This can start with the local family but should also include

3 . It is helpful to examine the epistemological foundations of opposing positions.  
Instead of people entrenching themselves in positions they consider to be based on  
theology, the examination of epistemology can reveal the bases of these positions and  
indicate the direction to move towards for facilitating the engagement of Scripture and

4\. The outcomes of contextualization should be recorded in the mother tongue.  
Later, they should be back-translated into a lingua franca for wider discussion. This  
becomes an important part of church history that can help guide contextualization in the  
future.  
5\. A local genre can facilitate contextualization, especially if it is attractive,  
engaging, memorable, discovery-oriented, and fits the cultural learning pattern. In the  
Builsa culture, the genre of proverbs fits these criteria. In the American culture, the genre  
of movies may be more suitable. These genres help people to learn how to ride the bike  
of contextualization. Once they become accustomed to the process, they can take on more  
difficult challenges.

hidden or deeper aspects of culture that are often overlooked. This can reveal root values  
or “excluded middle” issues that are difficult to identify otherwise. For the cultural  
insider, a genre such as proverbs can be a mirror reflecting back the soul of the culture.

complete view of culture can be obtained. For the cultural outsider, a genre like proverbs  
can be a window into the soul of culture revealing aspects of that culture that are hidden

7\. The mother tongue Scripture is the “interpreter of culture and tradition”  
\(Bediako 1999\:1\) since it provides the clearest revelation of God in culture. Discussions

9\. In order to communicate Christianity in a culture, the relevant and available  
symbols should be identified and appropriately used. These symbols are constructed and  
reconstructed by the participants in culture. Proverbs function as symbols in Builsa  
culture that have been created within the culture. As such, they can be used as “building  
blocks” to construct a Builsa Christian worldview, and then communicate this within  
Builsa culture. While the value of oral literature may easily be overlooked by those from  
literate cultures, they can be effective in communicating Christianity in the Builsa  
community that is composed of primary oral learners.

Training of Church Leaders and Missionaries  
The discussion so far has largely focused on grass-roots theologizing by  
promoting contextualization to address issues arising from the Builsa community. This  
has implications for the training of church workers, both cultural insiders and cultural  
outsiders. A few recommendations are offered here\:  
1\. Early missionary training and orientation should include exposure to the vast  
body of oral literature that includes proverbs. This should be included as a part of their  
language and culture learning. This includes those missionaries originating from outside  
the country as well as those missionaries from within the country that are not native to the  
host culture they are ministering in \(e.g., a Southern Ghanaian living in Buluk\). This  
exposure should include examples of some aesthetic aspects of oral literature. This is  
easily overlooked by missionaries from literate areas. This early exposure will help them  
later to understand and appreciate the culture, and then they can communicate more  
effectively within it.  
2\. Proverbs can be windows into the soul of Builsa culture. Missionaries should  
explore these windows in order to identify cultural values that are otherwise hard to  
identify. When compared to the missionaries’ home cultural values, this may identify  
areas where the missionary will likely have conflict with the cultural insiders. As a result,  
missionaries can make adjustments in order to become good news to the people.  
3\. Cultural outsiders can provide an etic perspective that is extremely helpful in  
the contextualization process. As one voice within the group, this voice can provide a  
helpful perspective at times.  
Reproduced with permission of the copyright owner. Further reproduction prohibited without permission. 265  
4\. By engaging another culture with Scripture, missionaries are likely to  
understand additional aspects of Scriptures that they overlooked before. They should  
humbly look for these insights within the culture. This forces the missionaries to be  
learners as well as teachers.  
5\. National Christian workers who receive a large amount of their formal  
theological education outside the context of their own language and culture should also  
be encouraged to study and analyze their own oral literature like proverbs in the light of  
Scripture. I will rejoice to see the day that Bible Colleges, Seminaries, etc. include this as  
a topic of study. The process described in the narrative could become a separate course  
by itself or it could be included in a wider course such as “Scripture and Culture.”  
6\. Part of the discipleship and leadership training in the church should include the  
study and analyzing of proverbs and other oral literature. Proverbs containing insults and  
negative attitudes should not be discarded. Rather, they should be critically evaluated  
using the process described above.  
7\. As proverbs are collected, church leaders and missionaries should meet  
together in order to prepare written materials in the mother tongue. These written  
materials could serve as teaching/preaching aids, as well as discipleship, evangelism, and  
counseling materials. The very process of church leaders meeting together to discuss how  
proverbs can be integrated into the church will encourage their use more. The written  
materials also allow the material to gain a wider usage. Periodically, church leaders from  
various backgrounds should also meet for group discussion in order to widen the  
perspective and reduce the risk of ethnocentrism.

Healey \(377\) states, “our examples of African stories and oral literature are like  
diamonds that need theologians and others to cut and polish more incisively to display  
their richness and beauty”. It is my hope that these diamonds will be unearthed by church  
leaders and missionaries, and then they be fashioned to give glory to God. The time is  
long overdue. In order for the church to overcome “split-level Christianity” and continue  
growing in Africa, the needs of Africa must be addressed with God’s answers and  
expressed in culturally relevant forms. I believe that the use of oral literature is one  
perspective that God has given the Builsa to facilitate the contextualization of  
Christianity in their culture. While it has often been overlooked in the past, it is right  
under our feet - and now we know it!  
On the Plane Home  
On the plane home from Buluk, I sit next to Femi, a Nigerian man on his way to  
the U.S. for the first time. We have a pleasant conversation, full of discoveries and  
surprises.  
“What have you been doing in Africa?” asks Femi.  
I casually reply, “I have been studying African proverbs and how they can help  
Africans to understand and communicate Christianity in a way that is fully African as  
well as fully Scriptural.”  
His eyes light up as he exclaims, “That is wonderful. I am coming to the U.S. to  
help start a branch of our church, the Redeemed Christian Church of God. I like proverbs  
and I am interested in how they can help us.”

# Buli Vocabulary  
*Bagi =* a horn that is filled with material \(often soil from the *tengbain* or *bogluk*\) in order  
to give the wearer protection. It is often worn around the neck like a charm or amulet.  
*Bano* = traditional soothsayer or diviner.  
*Bogluk* = Shrine or idol where sacrifices are offered  
*Buli* = language of the Builsa people.  
*Buluk* = land of the Builsa people.  
*Chichiruk* = a fairy. This being is not considered a human; rather, it is a spirit that is born  
in human form, often meant to cause harm to humans.  
*Jiuk* = Literally, “tail.” The tail of an animal can be carried by a Builsa in hopes of  
receiving protection.  
*Kisuk =* Taboo, forbidden action.  
*Kristobiik =* Christian. Literally, “Child of Christ.”  
*Kusung* = A hut in the front of the house used to greet visitors, to rest in the shade, etc.  
*Magsi* = to be proper, fitting, suitable, right  
*Naawen =* the High God who created all.  
*Naawen Wani* = God’s Word. This is used to refer to Scripture.  
*Saab =* Millet porridge that is a staple food for the Builsa.  
*Sakpak* = Witch who catches souls of people and kills them.  
*Tengbain* = Earth shrine  
*Teng Nyono* = caretaker of the local earth shrine  
*Tiim =* Medicine, or power source \(charm, amulet, juju, etc.\)
