The writing of this thesis has been a long and often arduous process. Several years have passed
since I first got interested in the topic of orality and literacy during my studies at Regent
College, Vancouver, where my eyes were opened to questions around the relationship between
culture and theology. I am still grateful to my professors there who made a lasting impression
on me and my thinking about God and life.

Researching and writing a dissertation while working full time would not have been possible
without the support, emotionally and financially, of my family, especially my parents. I know
that my father would rejoice with me � and perhaps he does, in heaven � over the completion
of this long process. My family, as well as my friends have often had to be patient when I said
"No" to them on different occasions � thank you!

Last but not least, I am grateful to my supervisors, professor dr. Wessel Stoker and professor
dr. Kees van der Kooi for their help and guidance over the years. Working together from a
distance has not been without its challenge, but without their comments, advice, repeated
encouragement, and the time they have given on the occasions of our meetings I would not have
been able to finish this project. Thank you very much!

Finally, Soli Deo Gloria to the God whose power is made perfect in weakness and who is able to
do immeasurably more than all we ask or imagine!
