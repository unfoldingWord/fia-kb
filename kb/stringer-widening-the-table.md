# Widening the Table\:  
# An Ethics of Accessible Exegesis and Interpretation  
Elisabeth Stringer and Stephen Stringer  
“We do not have the right to do exegesis.” The group of mother-tongue translators who made  
this statement had been translating for a few years into their local languages. They were  
attending a training with translation advisors and consultants — all of whom were quick to  
reassure the translators that they could, in fact, learn exegetical skills. But the translators, who  
had expert knowledge of their own language and culture, felt that they did not understand the  
exegetical resources they had seen well enough to make exegetical choices as they translated.  
Instead, they relied on their consultants to give them exegetical information, and defaulted to  
whatever interpretation of the Biblical text their consultants suggested.  
This paper is, in part, a response to these translators. They have an ethical right, we argue, to  
access the wealth of exegetical and theological information that exists in a format they can  
understand. The translation world should invite them to the table of exegesis and extend that  
table in order to include them. But beyond that, they have a right to do the work of exegesis  
and interpretation themselves, to build their own tables, and to bring the wealth of exegetical  
knowledge that work produces to the Bible translation world.  
We want to note that different communities may have different needs when it comes to  
accessible exegesis. Deaf communities, for example, may need highly visual exegetical material,  
and Deaf translators face unique challenges when it comes to issues of language deprivation  
and the need for technologies capable of video capture and editing. Communities that have  
experienced trauma in the form of violence, displacement, and extreme poverty may need to  
recognize and address their trauma in order to sustain healthy interpretative processes. This  
paper, however, will focus on the access needs of primary and secondary oral learners.  
We will begin by defining our terms and describing what we mean by “orality” and “oral  
theology.” We will then draw from both the biblical example of Yahweh making himself known  
and theories describing the ethics of power to make the case that access to knowledge is a core  
component of ethical exegesis. We then lay out what accessible exegesis looks like practically\: It  
looks like access to existing resources, reformatting those resources, and oral creation of  
exegesis. We end by situating our discussion in a real-life example of accessible exegetical  
material co-created with oral communities. While the case study demonstrates that accessible  
oral exegesis is possible, we hope the limits we identify inspire others to continue to innovate  
1 and search for new ways to both make existing exegesis accessible to oral audiences and to  
invite oral theologians into the process of creating exegetical knowledge and interpretation.  
# Defining orality and oral theology  
Some use the term orality interchangeably with illiteracy, or think of orality as a continuum,  
with literate, text-based learning on one end and oral-based learning on the other \(Ong 2012\).  
The field of oral studies, however, is vast and multi-disciplinary and ways of describing orality,  
its history, its culture, and its implications, are many \(Stringer 2023\). The terms primary oral  
learner and secondary oral learner, for example, illustrate some of the complexity and nuance  
that exists in oral communities by describing how individuals participate in orality differently.  
We use primary oral learner to describe individuals born into oral communities who continue to  
primarily use oral cognitive and reasoning processes. Primary oral learners tend to be less  
comfortable learning and creating knowledge through textual and literate means. Secondary  
oral leaners, however, are born into oral communities but pass through textuality and return to  
orality by choice or may have a higher reliance upon orality in certain contexts \(Carr 2010\).  
Secondary oral learners may prefer to learn and create knowledge through oral means but also  
can learn and create knowledge through textual and literate means. These terms serve as  
reminders that oral learners often weave in and out of textuality and can participate in literate  
and text-based skills and knowledge production.  
As a whole, we find it helpful to define orality as a variable constellation of tendencies and  
characteristics in the way people learn and think \(Madinger 2010; Steffen and Bjoraker 2020\).  
Different cultures and communities, and the individuals within them, may have different oral  
characteristics or present the same characteristics differently \(Street 1995; Bush 2016\). For the  
purposes of this paper, we focus on three characteristics of orality that tend to also be present  
in oral theology and hermeneutics\: Embodied epistemology, narrative rationality, and  
communal semiotics.  
Oral learners often learn best through embodied epistemological methodologies — methods of  
learning that are multimodal, multisensory, embodied, experiential, and repetitive \(Steffen and  
Bjoraker 2020; Fanta-Vagenshtein and Chen 2009; Rynkiewich 2007\). Many Bible translation  
processes, especially in Oral Bible Translation \(OBT\), use methods of internalization that invite  
oral translators to study Scripture or theology in multi-modal and embodied ways.  
Oral communities tend to rationalize and interpret information through narrative rationality.  
These communities make sense of concepts by situating them within wider narratives, and  
reason through those concepts by connecting them to story, narrative, and real-life scenarios  
2 \(Ong 2012; Ricoeur 2006\). Didactic information is understood within the discursive context of  
the narrative and metanarrative surrounding that information \(Stringer 2023\).  
Finally, oral learners tend towards communal semiotics. Historically, oral communities have not  
relied upon text to hold and communicate information. Instead, oral cultures developed  
systems for holding onto memory and information collectively. They communicate those  
memories and that information collectively, often through repetitive oral recitation and  
intricate storytelling and singing traditions \(Kelber 2006\). The interpretation and meaning-  
making of that information also happens in community, and individual interpretations are  
checked against communal memory and knowledge \(Yong 2017\).  
These three characteristics describe how oral communities tend to learn, understand, and  
interpret knowledge — and how those communities tend to create hermeneutical and  
theological knowledge. Missiological and theological circles sometimes overlook or dismiss oral  
theology because their own systems of learning and knowledge creation tend to rely on literate  
and text-based systems, often rooted within western knowledge schema and cultural  
determinations of what counts as sound knowledge. But oral communities, and the primary and  
secondary oral learners within them, not only can but do regularly interpret and exegete  
Scripture. These communities, often based in the global south, learn through embodied  
engagement with Scripture, rationalize scriptural concepts by situating them in the cultural  
narrative and the metanarrative of Scripture, and interpret Scripture through established  
communal frameworks of meaning-making \(Stringer 2023\).  
# The biblical example of accessible exegesis  
If we define ethics as the moral principles that govern the way we move through the world,  
then we should look towards the Bible and the patterns within it to discover those principles.  
Throughout the Bible, Yahweh continually makes himself understandable to us. From the very  
beginning, Yahweh reveals himself through his creation. He makes humans in his image, and  
through creation humans “can clearly see his invisible qualities — his eternal power and divine  
nature” \(Romans 1\:20 New Living Translation; Genesis 1\:27 NLT\). Even after the fall breaks  
Adam and Eve’s close relationship with Yahweh, he constantly describes and reveals himself to  
his chosen people. He contextualizes himself when he speaks to Abram, using understandable  
cultural rituals from the surrounding regions to communicate his promises to Abram \(Genesis  
15\:4-21 NLT; Hamilton 1990\). He tells Moses his name, Yahweh, in an intimate and profound  
revelation \(Exodus 3\:14 NLT\). He constantly sends messengers to his people not only to tell  
them what he wants, but also to reveal his heart and himself to them through figurative  
language, story, and embodied example — just think of Yahweh describing himself as a mother  
bear and asking Hosea to marry Gomer in the book of Hosea \(Hosea 13\:8 NLT; Hosea 1\:2 NLT\).  
3 But the quintessential way in which Yahweh reveals himself to us and makes himself knowable  
by us is when “The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us,” and Yahweh comes  
to humanity in the human person of Jesus \(John 1\:4 NLT\). Through Jesus, Yahweh translated  
himself into a form that we could understand. Through Jesus, we can have a relationship with  
Yahweh — access to Yahweh that is not mediated but a direct communion with the eternal  
communion of the triune God \(Hebrews 10\:19-22 NLT; Ephesians 2\:18 NLT\).  
This clear pattern should serve as a biblical example of why exegesis — the explanation and  
interpretation of Scripture — should be accessible. Yahweh makes himself accessible to all and  
invites all into interactive relationship with him as revealed in his word and the Word. In the  
same way, Scripture, the divine discourse between the triune God and his people, should be  
accessible to all and everyone should be invited into interactive relationship with it.  
# Ethical exegesis and transformative power  
Every relationship includes power dynamics \(Foucault 1977\). Power, in turn, is intricately bound  
up with knowledge — it determines who gets to create knowledge and make decisions  
\(Batliwala 2019\). Power shapes how and what knowledge is created, and knowledge can either  
perpetuate, or serve as a site of resistance to, oppressive power \(Foucault 1977; Gaventa 2006\).  
It is beyond the scope of this paper to fully analyze the complex power relationships within  
Bible translation — a topic that deserves more attention. But in the case of exegesis, examining  
power means asking the questions\: Who gets to exegete? And who gets to make interpretive  
choices?  
The scholar Srilatha Batliwala provides a helpful framework through which to view power. She  
describes five expressions of power relationships. The two most relevant to us are “power  
over” and “power to.” In power over, individuals or groups have direct or indirect control over  
others, especially when it comes to making decisions. Power to, on the other hand, describes  
when individuals or groups have agency and are capable of independently acting towards  
collective or personal goals for themselves \(Batliwala 2019\). Other power theorists have  
described power to as “transformative power,” a form of power that allows individuals and  
communities to transform themselves and others \(Wartenberg 1990; Sardenberg 2016\). In her  
discussion of power movements among women in the global south, Sardenberg further  
nuances transformative power. She describes “liberal empowerment” as power movements  
that see empowerment, or power to, as instruments for pursuing goals often based on  
individual interests. She contrasts this to “liberating empowerment,” movements that see  
empowerment as instruments of communal transformation, ends in and of themselves, and a  
method of challenging unequal power dynamics \(Sardenberg 2016\).  
4 Exegesis that is hard for oral learners to access results in power over rather than power to  
dynamics. When one group, in this case text-based learners, holds an outsized influence in  
accessing and creating knowledge, they will tend to have more power to make interpretive  
translation choices on behalf of the language and culture in which the translation is situated.  
Not only does this result in a poorer translation that does not draw on the expertise of oral  
theologians, it also can deny oral theologians the ability to do their own interpretation,  
exegesis, and theologizing. Instead, we call for a transformative approach to power dynamics  
within exegesis. Ethical exegesis needs to be built on power to relationships in which oral  
theologians create their own knowledge and make their own interpretive exegetical choices. It  
needs to be, in other words, accessible.  
# Access to the table  
What do we mean by accessibility? For the purposes of this paper, we will focus on three  
realms of accessibility\: Physical access, accessible formats, and participatory access. The first of  
these forms of access, physical access, is the easiest to immediately implement. Bible  
translation organizations and translation consultants draw on exegetical resources built on  
centuries of theological debate, thought, and careful interpretation. Oral communities,  
however, face physical barriers to accessing that existing knowledge.  
Mother-tongue translators often live in areas with poor infrastructure, lack funding for their  
projects, or do not speak English as their language of wider communication. A respected and  
comprehensive Bible commentary that requires an internet connection to access, money to  
purchase, and academic English to understand is out of their reach. Poor infrastructure means  
translators may not have access to the internet or the technological literacy to easily use the  
internet. Some teams face enormous learning curves when attempting to use computers and  
phones to participate in translation drafting and editing processes. A lack of funding also  
complicates that participation, as purchasing subscriptions or resources for themselves may be  
unattainable for some teams. And even if teams have good infrastructure and funding, the  
exegetical resources available are primarily in English — in our own work resourcing translation  
teams, the top piece of feedback we receive is that teams cannot use our material or easily  
understand it because our material is primarily in English \(Word Collective 2024a\).  
When teams face barriers to physically accessing existing exegetical discourse and knowledge,  
they rely on outsiders — translation consultants, Bible translation organizations, western  
donors, denominational leaders in their country, or other stakeholders — to bridge that gap by  
providing funding, access to resources, and interpretation and translation of those resources.  
This means if funding suddenly gets withdrawn the project cannot be completed; a consultant  
5 takes knowledge with them when they leave a team; and technological problems slow a team’s  
progress. When it comes to exegesis, it means that teams must rely on an outside exegete  
because they do not have the theological context necessary to exegete themselves.  
Addressing these physical barriers to access can be complex and frustrating. But encouraging  
work is already taking place. Several digital platforms providing translation resources, for  
example, have worked to make their interfaces user-friendly and available offline. The ETEN  
Innovation Lab’s Aquifer goes a step further, specifically seeking to remove paywalls and  
language barriers for translators and churches worldwide. It provides a centralized, curated,  
and freely available repository of resources through its API and associated Bible Well app.  
Furthermore, the Aquifer team walks interested translation organizations through how to make  
their material freely available through specific licensees, and seeks to translate that material  
into major gateway languages \(Aquifer\).  
# Widening the table  
Making exegesis accessible to oral communities also looks like re-shaping existing exegetical  
knowledge and processes into oral formats. The corpus of exegetical material and methods of  
interpretation used by Bible translators tends towards textual and literate knowledge creation  
and dissemination. Commentaries, media, resources, and translation software mostly include  
text-based resources. This Bible Translation Conference creates conversations on exegesis,  
shapes the type of exegetical material created, and directly impacts on-the-ground translation  
projects. But this conference creates that knowledge through highly academic, textual papers  
and presentations based entirely on those texts.  
Making exegesis accessible might look like re-imagining both the format and process of  
exegesis. On a surface level, this could look like putting exegetical materials into audio formats  
so primary oral learners with low levels of comfort around text can access information in a  
format they can easily understand. OBT projects have already started to do this. They invite  
translators to create translation drafts aurally, and aim for an audio-based translation — the  
format oral translators and their communities can best understand and utilize. Digital platforms  
for translation have begun to provide translators with tools to create and comment on  
translation drafts aurally — AVTT, Render, and SIL’s Audio Project Manager make it possible for  
translation teams to draft, community check, and edit their translations in an audio format.  
Translation projects could also go a step further and re-format exegetical material and  
processes of exegesis in ways that incorporate embodied epistemology, narrative rationality,  
and communal semiotics. This would move from audio-accessible \(aural\) exegetical materials  
towards oral exegesis. Again, some of this work has already begun, especially in the realms of  
6 OBT and church-based translation approaches such as church-based Bible translation \(CBBT\)  
and church and community based Bible translation \(CCBBT\). Internalization processes used by  
many of these projects invite translators to learn exegetical information through narrative and  
experiential learning. The push towards multi-model information has also opened more  
avenues for oral learners to find understandable and engaging information. Finally, church-  
based bible translation \(CBBT\) training focuses on communicating translation processes to oral  
communities through embodied activities, repetition, narrative, and communal discussion and  
decision making \(Innovation Lab 2024\).  
# Building new tables  
Oral learners and theologians, however, do not need to be merely accommodated. Their insight  
and expertise needs to be recognized, supported, and utilized by the global church. Instead of  
just adding seats to the existing table of exegesis by lowering barriers to and re-formatting  
exegetical knowledge, we need to invite oral theologians to build new tables. Practically, this  
means supporting and advocating for oral theologians to participate in exegesis, and then to  
participate in the creation and dissemination of exegetical knowledge. It involves identifying,  
describing, and challenging the power dynamics at play in translation projects by asking who  
has the power to make exegetical and interpretive decisions and advocating for oral  
theologians to fully participate in that decision-making.  
# Oral creation of exegetical materials and theological interpretation  
FIA\: An attempt at co-creation of exegetical materials  
What might supporting oral communities as they build their own tables of exegesis look like?  
The following is a brief case study of our own attempts to co-create exegetical resources for  
oral communities through the FIA project. It describes the methods we employed to do so, the  
results we have seen so far, and ends with suggestions for improvement and further expansion  
of such co-creation. We hope that the impact we have seen through FIA demonstrates that it is  
possible to co-create exegetical materials and processes with oral communities and that the  
limitations of the project invite others to imagine new ways to widen and build new tables.  
FIA began as a response to two CCBBT projects that began in Papua New Guinea\: The United  
Church Papuan Islands Region \(UCPIR\) and BATTLE Inc. Translators in these projects were  
primary and secondary oral learners, and needed accessible exegetical material in order to  
draft their translations. Wycliffe Global Partnerships and Every Tribe Every Nation \(ETEN\)’s  
Innovation Lab supported both projects and approached FIA to help them create such  
materials.  
7 When making the first draft of exegetical materials for the book of Mark, the FIA team drew on  
their own knowledge of and experience among oral communities. FIA’s original format was  
developed by a translation consultant who lived and worked with oral communities for over 20  
years and an orality expert who is a secondary oral learner himself. Originally, FIA consisted of  
five steps for each biblical pericope, with each step focusing on a different component of  
exegesis. The steps, once turned into audio, were meant for groups of translators to experience  
together, and included discussion questions, activities, and guidance for making translation  
choices. They were meant for a translation team to use as a community. The steps situated  
each passage in the wider biblical metanarrative, and provided information about the culture,  
linguistics, geography, discourse, flow of logic, and emotions of each passage. They guided  
translators to internalize that information through embodied, experiential practices. The  
materials also included as much media as possible, to give users a real-world, concrete example  
of how biblical objects and geography were used. However, we knew that truly accessible oral  
materials need to come from oral communities themselves. We attempted to co-create  
material mainly by implementing rigorous feedback and testing loops.  
The two CCBBT projects in Papua New Guinea used our original material for Mark as we  
produced it. In 2023, the projects invited us to learn from them. Over the course of two weeks,  
we gathered feedback on how translation teams had utilized and adapted FIA. We observed  
five teams use FIA and interviewed over 20 stakeholders, including translators, team leaders,  
translation advisors, UCPIR church leaders, and community reviewers. We explicitly asked how  
we needed to change our material to meet the needs of the mostly primary oral learners in the  
translation teams. Translators told us they used FIA not only as a resource but also as a drafting  
process. Because we built the resources to be learned and discussed in community, translators  
had begun to make interpretative and translation choices while learning the exegetical material  
in FIA. Based on their recommendation, we added a sixth step that explicitly guides translators  
through the process of articulating a first translation draft. Our other scripts also changed as  
translators suggested ways to make the format clearer, recommended we include more  
repetition and more information in the scripts, and suggested that we include more  
opportunities for discussion and embodiment. The translators also changed the name of our  
resource — instead of calling our steps “CBBT exegetical resources,” teams coined  
“familiarization, internalization, and articulation” based on the three main components they  
saw at play \(Stringer 2023\).  
We continue to seek feedback on our materials from oral communities, building processes of  
iteration into FIA creation whenever possible. This primarily happens through focus groups of  
primary oral learners, to whom we present FIA material and seek feedback and input. We have  
held focus groups on\: Text-to-speech versions of our scripts, AI generated images, the staging  
8 of photos of objects, the staging of photos of geography, the format of maps, the format of  
illustrations, the length and order of FIA steps, the format of genre-specific material, and more.  
We also reach out to translation projects using FIA materials, ask to observe their translators’  
use of FIA, hold interviews with their translators and facilitators to collate qualitative data that  
allows us to find patterns and implement changes to our materials accordingly. As much as  
possible, we prioritize the feedback of oral communities above our own instincts and goals. For  
example, text-to-speech technologies would greatly speed up the process of turning our  
written scripts for each step into audios, but three oral focus groups reported that they  
preferred human over AI voiced scripts, even when the audio quality of the AI-voiced scripts  
was superior. Based on that feedback, we continue to create human-voiced scripts \(Word  
Collective 2024b\).  
The impact of FIA co-creation  
Initial impact evaluations of FIA resources seem to demonstrate that FIA increases oral  
translators’ understanding of exegetical material. In a survey of six team leaders of six OBT  
translation teams in Nepal, all six said that FIA helped their teams of oral mother-tongue  
translators understand the Scripture they were translating. When asked why they used FIA, one  
team leader answered\: “It’s very much helpful to make the passage clear” \(Word Collective  
2024a\). In Kenya, 13 OBT translation projects report that FIA increases their translators’ ability  
to draft Scripture, and translation consultants working the projects report that those teams  
tend to produce high-quality first drafts \(Word Collective 2025b\). One year after we visited the  
CCBBT teams in Papua New Guinea, those translation teams continue to report that FIA  
increases their exegetical knowledge. A third-party evaluation of the projects described the  
communities’ use of FIA as a driving force of transformation within the communities  
themselves because FIA allowed the community to deeply connect to Scripture in a way they  
could understand. The evaluation cites one community as saying\: “FIA fits very well with our  
culture — we keep history in our mind. Stories are the way our history is kept, and you have to  
act on it” \(Global Partnerships 2024\).  
There are also indications that FIA helps translators feel empowered to communicate and  
assert their exegetical interpretations of Scripture, sometimes without the explicit guidance of a  
text-based, literate exegete. For example, we have reports of teams noticing what they  
considered interpretative exegetical errors in their source texts and informing their team  
leaders, or outside exegetical consultants, that they decided to make different translation  
choices based on their own exegesis. In three different sign language projects translators noted  
that the sign for alabaster jar in their source text indicated a large jar, but pointed out that,  
according to the information in FIA, the jar was probably much smaller. All three teams decided  
they should choose a different sign in their translations \(Stringer 2023\). A team in Papua New  
Guinea pointed out to their team leader that the Bible in their language of wider  
9 communication used a word for wilderness in Mark 1\:12 that more accurately meant jungle.  
The team informed their team leader that, based on their own exegesis, jungle was an  
inadequate translation because one can easily find food in Papuan New Guinea jungles, and it is  
important to communicate that Jesus could not easily find food in the wilderness. Through their  
own communal discussion and interpretation of the passage, the team opted to use a short  
descriptive phrase for wilderness in their translation draft \(Stringer 2023\).  
The limitations of FIA  
Despite positive initial feedback from oral communities, the FIA attempt at co-creation is  
flawed. To begin with, over 500 translation teams, spread across more than 13 countries, use  
FIA \(Word Collective 2025a\). Since characteristics of orality present themselves differently in  
different cultural contexts, it is impossible for FIA to reflect all characteristics of orality.  
Different oral communities need different resources. Ideally, re-formatting and creating new  
exegetical material for oral communities would be highly contextualized to the ways of  
exegeting and theologizing within each community.  
Furthermore, while the end product reflects oral characteristics and tendencies and feedback  
from primary and secondary oral learners has been instrumental in shaping FIA, the practical  
production of knowledge in FIA itself remains highly text-based. FIA content developers are  
either text-based learners or secondary oral learners —they must be able to understand highly-  
regarded text-based exegetical resources in order to re-shape those resources into oral forms.  
This means that developers tend to work individually, not communally; they do not tend to  
embody the exegetical information they use to write materials; and they write and edit their  
scripts in text before converting them into audio formats. While FIA seems to help empower  
oral end-users to exegete themselves, a true shift of power dynamics in Bible translation would  
require oral communities to fully embody the process of creating new exegetical knowledge.  
# An invitation to imagination  
We hope we have begun to provide an answer to the oral translators who said\: “We do not  
have the right to do exegesis.  
” The biblical example of Yahweh making himself known to us, and  
explorations of the concept of transformative power relationships, demonstrate that these oral  
translators have an ethical right to exegesis. We argue that they should have access to existing  
exegesis in formats they can best understand and that the interpretive and exegetical  
knowledge they build themselves is invaluable to the world of Bible translation.  
We believe that the FIA case study helps bolster our argument by demonstrating that it is  
possible to co-create accessible oral exegetical information, and that oral communities will use  
that information to exegete and interpret Scripture themselves. Ultimately, however, FIA only  
gestures towards building new tables of oral exegesis. Within the Bible translation world, there  
10 is still much work to be done to find, support, and draw from oral exegesis created through oral  
means by oral communities.  
We invite Bible translation organizations, consultants, and team members to ask yourselves\:  
How can we make our resources available and accessible to oral communities in the global  
church? How can we invite oral theologians to speak into our processes of exegesis and  
interpretation?
